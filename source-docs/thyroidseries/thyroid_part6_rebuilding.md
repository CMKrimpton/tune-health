# THE THYROID SERIES — Part 6
# Rebuilding the Thyroid
### *Microbiome interventions, AI diagnostics, polygenic risk scoring, precision medicine, and what the thyroid of 2035 looks like*

---

Medicine moves in two speeds. The first is the speed of discovery — the pace at which mechanisms are elucidated, clinical associations are established, and the underlying biology of a condition is finally understood with enough resolution to act on it intelligently. The second is the speed of adoption — the pace at which that understanding reaches clinical practice, changes what physicians do in exam rooms, and alters what patients receive.

The gap between these two speeds is not incidental. It is the product of institutional inertia, reimbursement structures, guideline conservatism, and the reasonable, if sometimes excessive, caution of a profession that has been burned by enthusiastic adoption of incompletely validated therapies. In thyroid medicine, as the previous five parts have documented, this gap is wide. The science has moved considerably further than the practice in almost every domain covered in this series.

This final part attempts something different: to examine not just where the research is now, but where the convergence of emerging technologies, precision medicine frameworks, and microbiome science is taking thyroid care — and what "good" thyroid medicine looks like when it is allowed to reflect what we actually know.

---

## The Microbiome Frontier: From Association to Intervention

The gut-thyroid axis, introduced in Part 1 and examined in depth in Part 2, is transitioning from an association-based research agenda to an intervention-based one. The mechanistic evidence is sufficiently established to support clinical trials of microbiome-targeted therapies. Those trials are now underway.

The IMITHOT trial — the Improving Microbiome in Hypothyroidism by FMT trial, currently running in the Netherlands — is the first randomized controlled trial examining fecal microbiota transplantation as a therapeutic intervention in subclinical autoimmune hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's thyroiditis). It is a double-blind, randomized controlled design: FMT from healthy donors versus sham procedure, in patients with established Hashimoto's thyroiditis on levothyroxine. The primary outcome is change in thyroid antibody levels; secondary outcomes include TSH, T4, T3, and patient-reported quality of life.

The scientific rationale for this trial is now solid. Animal model data — mice receiving FMT from hypothyroid human donors develop reduced tight junction expression in the colon and increased circulating LPS, confirming that the dysbiotic state is transferable and causally affects thyroid immune biology. Conversely, FMT from healthy donors in animal models of Hashimoto's thyroiditis reduces thyroid antibody levels and attenuates the inflammatory phenotype.

The FMT approach is the most direct test of the gut-thyroid axis hypothesis. But it is not the only intervention in the pipeline. Specific probiotic strains have been investigated with increasing rigor. Lactiplantibacillus plantarum 299v — a well-characterized probiotic strain with documented effects on intestinal barrier integrity and anti-inflammatory cytokine production — has shown preliminary evidence of benefit in autoimmune thyroid disease in small trials. Bifidobacterium longum combined with methimazole in Graves' disease patients reduced TSH receptor antibody levels versus methimazole alone in a 2021 randomized controlled trial. These are small studies requiring replication, but they represent the first controlled evidence that microbiome-targeted approaches directly affect the autoimmune process in thyroid disease.

The prebiotic angle is less headline-ready but potentially more scalable. Dietary fiber fermentation by gut bacteria produces the short-chain fatty acids — primarily butyrate — that maintain intestinal barrier integrity, regulate T-cell differentiation, and suppress pro-inflammatory signaling. A Mediterranean diet, rich in diverse fiber sources, directly supports the butyrate-producing bacterial genera that are depleted in Hashimoto's and Graves' disease patients. The evidence for Mediterranean diet's anti-inflammatory effects is robust. Whether those effects translate specifically to reduced thyroid autoimmunity in prospective trials has not been formally established, but the mechanistic pathway is clear, the intervention is safe, and the broader health benefits justify the recommendation independent of thyroid-specific evidence.

The emerging field of postbiotics — specific bacterial metabolites, including butyrate formulations and other SCFA preparations, administered directly — may offer a more targeted approach than probiotic supplementation, bypassing the colonization uncertainty inherent in probiotic interventions. Whether orally administered butyrate reaches the colonic concentrations relevant to tight junction maintenance is still under investigation; enteric-coated formulations are being developed specifically to address this limitation.

## Artificial Intelligence and the Diagnostic Revolution

The AI-assisted thyroid nodule assessment technology described in Part 1's overview is advancing quickly enough that a dedicated assessment is warranted.

Deep learning systems trained on large ultrasound image datasets are now achieving performance on thyroid nodule malignancy classification that matches or exceeds experienced radiologists on retrospective testing datasets. The S-Detect for Thyroid system, which uses Samsung's AI architecture to classify nodules against European TI-RADS criteria, was evaluated in a 2025 clinical trial involving 20 participants ranging from medical students to experienced ultrasound practitioners scanning five patients with thyroid nodules. The AI system consistently improved classification accuracy across all experience levels — and did so by guiding less experienced operators toward correct characterization decisions, reducing the interobserver variability that has long been the Achilles heel of thyroid ultrasound risk stratification.

A September 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis in Frontiers in Oncology examined AI diagnostic models for thyroid nodules across multiple published studies, finding generally high sensitivity and specificity for malignancy detection. The important caveat the review identifies — operator-dependent variability in AI-assisted assessment — is a genuine limitation. AI systems that perform well when operated by trained users in academic centers may not transfer seamlessly to community settings, where operator training and image quality are more variable.

The more transformative potential of AI in thyroid medicine may not be in real-time ultrasound assistance but in integrating multimodal data streams. Emerging systems are being developed that combine ultrasound imaging features, molecular biopsy results, patient demographics, TSH trajectories, genomic data, and clinical history into composite risk prediction models. The TIRONOD Decision Support System, published in 2025, represents an early iteration: a multi-input clinical decision support tool for thyroid nodule management that integrates imaging, cytology, and clinical parameters. The limitation of current models is their training data — predominantly from academic medical centers in high-income countries, with limited representation of diverse populations and community clinical environments.

A multimodal large language model evaluated as a classifier of thyroid nodule malignancy risk — essentially asking a large AI model to interpret not just images but the entire clinical context — was published in JMIR Formative Research in August 2025. Its performance was competitive with subspecialty radiologists on the test dataset, but the study population was small and the application to real-world clinical deployment remains preliminary.

What these developments collectively suggest is a near-term future where thyroid nodule risk stratification is routinely AI-assisted, interobserver variability is substantially reduced, and the threshold for biopsy — currently applied inconsistently and often excessively — is calibrated against integrated risk estimates rather than single-modality ultrasound scoring.

## Genetics, Polygenic Risk Scores, and the Personalized Thyroid

The large-scale genome-wide association studies now characterizing the genetic architecture of thyroid disease represent a shift from treating thyroid conditions as dichotomous (present or absent) to treating them as polygenic traits distributed across a population.

A 2025 analysis reported in Nature Reviews Endocrinology identified hundreds of genetic loci associated with hypothyroidism and thyroid hormone levels. The magnitude of this finding is significant: it means that the predisposition to thyroid dysfunction is distributed across many genetic variants, each with small individual effect, but collectively accounting for a substantial portion of population variation in thyroid hormone levels and autoimmune thyroid disease susceptibility.

Polygenic risk scores (PRS) — aggregate measures of an individual's genetic burden across all relevant loci — can predict who will develop thyroid disease years before clinical symptoms appear. This has immediate implications for screening and preventive strategy: high-PRS individuals could be targeted for earlier and more intensive thyroid monitoring, for proactive iodine status optimization, and for more aggressive management of modifiable environmental exposures.

For treatment, the DIO2 genetics story from Part 1 is the leading edge of what personalized thyroid medicine will look like. The Thr92Ala DIO2 polymorphism is already identified in academic endocrinology settings as a clinical consideration for patients failing to respond adequately to levothyroxine monotherapy. As genetic testing costs continue to fall and as the clinical evidence base for DIO2-guided treatment decisions continues to accumulate, genotyping at the DIO2 locus — and potentially the MCT10 transporter locus — will likely become standard before initiating thyroid hormone replacement therapy in patients where combination T4/T3 therapy might be appropriate.

The population-level implication is equally significant. If DIO2 Thr92Ala homozygotes — roughly 12–15% of the European-ancestry population — show increased Alzheimer's disease risk attributable to chronic localized brain hypothyroidism despite normal serum TSH, then identifying these individuals early and ensuring their cerebral T3 supply is adequate represents a potentially important avenue for neurodegeneration prevention. This is speculative at this stage — the causal evidence for the Alzheimer's association is not yet established — but the mechanistic pathway (ER stress in glial cells, localized hypothyroidism, gene expression profiles consistent with neurodegeneration) is coherent enough to be formally investigated.

## The T3 Question, Resolved

The liothyronine debate has raged in endocrinology for three decades, largely because the relevant clinical trials were not designed to detect what they were looking for — benefit in genetically stratified subgroups — and instead measured average effects across genetically heterogeneous populations.

The emerging clinical framework for T3 supplementation — informed by the 2024 UK Biobank analysis, the WATTS study genetics, the DIO2 mechanistic research, and the JCI mouse model data — looks approximately like this:

For patients with levothyroxine-treated hypothyroidism who report persistent symptoms (fatigue, cognitive fog, mood disturbance) despite TSH normalization, the clinical evaluation should include: measurement of free T3 and reverse T3 (to identify functional T3 deficiency in the peripheral compartment); DIO2 and MCT10 genotyping; assessment of selenium, iron, and iodine status; and evaluation of gut health and chronic inflammatory status. This is not yet standard practice. It represents the practice ceiling that the evidence currently supports.

For patients with the DIO2 Ala/Ala genotype, or the combined DIO2/MCT10 variant pattern, combination T4/T3 therapy — or desiccated thyroid extract — is now backed by mechanistic rationale, genetic association data, and clinical observation in a way that justifies a formal treatment trial in individual patients, carefully monitored with regular T3 and TSH assessment to avoid over-replacement.

The European Thyroid Association guidelines currently classify T4/T3 combination therapy as an "experimental" option to be considered only in patients whose symptoms persist despite TSH normalization. This classification — "experimental" — will likely migrate toward "conditionally recommended for genetically stratified patients" as the genetic evidence accumulates further and as the clinical trials designed to test this question (with genetic stratification as a primary variable) are completed.

Peat's clinical intuition — that the standard levothyroxine monotherapy fails a significant subset of patients, that T3 availability is the operative variable, and that measuring TSH alone is insufficient — has been retrospectively vindicated by the molecular biology of DIO2. The intuition preceded the mechanism. The mechanism is now established. The practice change is lagging.

## The Sleep-Thyroid-Aging Triangle

Part 2 noted the emerging research on sleep and thyroid function. For a complete picture of what future thyroid management will integrate, this intersection deserves its own examination.

TSH secretion is profoundly circadian, with the secretory peak occurring between midnight and 4 AM. This nocturnal TSH surge is the primary driver of the 20–40% higher TSH values seen in early morning blood draws compared to afternoon draws — a source of diagnostic inconsistency that is rarely addressed by standardizing draw timing. More fundamentally, the circadian regulation of TSH secretion means that chronic circadian disruption — from shift work, artificial light exposure at night, and social jetlag — continuously blunts the physiological TSH peak and, over time, alters the pituitary-thyroid feedback dynamics.

In aging, TSH levels rise while T3 levels fall slightly. This pattern — higher TSH, lower T3 — has been hypothesized to represent either a protective adaptation (conserving metabolic rate in aging tissues that would otherwise be metabolically overdriven) or a pathological loss of regulatory precision in the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis. The answer matters enormously for whether age-related "subclinical hypothyroidism" in the elderly should be treated.

A compelling longitudinal analysis from the Whickham cohort, published in December 2024, found that TSH increased from a mean of 1.94 to 2.18 mIU/L over 7.8 years of follow-up in older individuals, while free T4 remained stable and free T3 decreased slightly. Thyroid antibodies decreased over the same period. When age-appropriate reference ranges were applied, the rate of subclinical hypothyroidism diagnosis fell from 9.0% to 2% using standard versus age-specific ranges. The implication is not just diagnostic recalibration — it is that the TSH rise in the elderly may represent normal physiology, and treating it pharmacologically may produce harm (cardiac arrhythmia, bone loss, anxiety) by suppressing a compensatory adaptation.

Interestingly, research on Italian centenarians found that older people from longevity-associated families had lower T4 and T3 levels and higher TSH levels — and so did the children and nieces and nephews of these centenarians. This suggests the thyroid phenotype associated with longevity may actually include modestly elevated TSH, not the lower TSH values associated with peak metabolic rate in younger adults. The bioenergetic logic — that lower metabolic rate in aging may reduce oxidative stress and slow certain degenerative processes — is not without theoretical basis, even if it conflicts with Peat's emphasis on maintaining high thyroid function throughout life. The truth here is probably age-dependent: optimizing T3 availability in working-age adults may have different risk-benefit calculus than aggressively treating TSH elevations in octogenarians.

## The Thyroid of 2035: A Speculative but Evidence-Based Picture

Integrating the trajectories established across the preceding five parts, the thyroid medicine of a decade from now will likely look like this:

**Screening** will be more comprehensive and more targeted simultaneously. Universal first-trimester thyroid screening for pregnant women — TSH, free T4, TPO antibodies — will be standard, driven by the accumulating evidence linking maternal thyroid status to autism risk, miscarriage, and cognitive outcomes in offspring. For the general population, polygenic risk scoring will identify high-burden individuals for earlier and more intensive monitoring. Age-specific reference ranges will be standard, reducing overdiagnosis in the elderly.

**Diagnosis** will be multimodal. The TSH + free T4 panel will be augmented with free T3, reverse T3, and antibody testing as the standard thyroid workup, not as an extended panel requiring specialist justification. DIO2 genotyping will be routinely incorporated into the workup for symptomatic patients who are candidates for thyroid hormone replacement. AI-assisted ultrasound will be standard of care for nodule assessment, reducing biopsy rates and improving malignancy detection accuracy.

**Treatment** will be personalized. The DIO2 genotype will inform the choice between levothyroxine monotherapy, combination T4/T3 therapy, and desiccated thyroid extract. Selenium status will be assessed and corrected as standard practice before and during Hashimoto's management. Microbiome-targeted adjuncts — specific probiotics, FMT for refractory cases, dietary modifications — will be integrated into autoimmune thyroid disease management as the clinical trial data mature.

**Environmental medicine** will be part of thyroid care. The clinical history for thyroid patients will include PFAS exposure assessment (military service, firefighting, contaminated water districts), dietary iodine intake, BPA and phthalate exposure reduction counseling, and sleep quality evaluation. These are not alternative medicine additions. They are physiologically justified interventions for a disease whose upstream drivers are increasingly understood.

**Cancer management** will be governed by molecular risk rather than histological staging. Surveillance for low-risk microcarcinomas will be the default, not surgery. Thermal ablation will be broadly available. Genetic profiling at diagnosis will guide adjuvant therapy decisions. Targeted therapies for advanced disease will extend survival further.

## Closing: What the Thyroid Deserves

The thyroid gland weighs less than an ounce. It sits in a location most people could not identify on their own body. It produces two hormones that are, directly or through conversion, responsible for the metabolic rate of every cell in the body, the architecture of the fetal brain, the integrity of the cardiovascular system, the function of the gut, and — via T3's role in mitochondrial energy production — the basic biological vitality that determines how alert, warm, energetic, and cognitively capable you feel on any given day.

It is disrupted, at sub-clinical levels, by industrial chemicals now present in the blood and tissue of virtually every person alive. It is attacked, in tens of millions of people, by autoimmune processes whose upstream origins in the gut are only now being systematically investigated. It is tested with a single indirect proxy that cannot see what is happening inside cells. It is treated, when treatment is given at all, with a single hormone that a significant genetic minority cannot adequately convert to the active form their tissues need.

The field of thyroidology is not in crisis. It is, in the most honest sense, catching up. The tools to do better exist. The evidence base to act on them is accumulating, faster now than at any prior point in the history of this discipline. What remains is the will — of institutions, of reimbursement systems, of practitioners, and of patients who understand enough to demand a more complete account of what their thyroid actually needs.

This series has tried to provide the foundation for that demand.
