# THE THYROID SERIES — Part 3
# The Poisoned Well
### *The industrial chemicals conducting a slow-motion assault on thyroid function — and the regulatory failures making it permanent*

---

Here is a useful thought experiment. Imagine you wanted to design a chemical that would be maximally effective at disrupting thyroid function across an entire population. You would want it to be stable enough to persist in the environment for decades without breaking down. You would want it to bioaccumulate in human tissue, so that the longer a person lives, the more of it they carry. You would want it to be structurally similar to thyroid hormones, so it could compete for binding to transport proteins and receptors. You would want it lipid-soluble, so it could cross the placenta and enter breast milk — ensuring that prenatal and neonatal exposure would occur at the most developmentally vulnerable windows. And you would want it to be so commercially useful that it would be produced and dispersed for decades before the biological consequences became impossible to ignore.

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — PFAS — fit this description with the precision of a designed system, though they were produced for the far more mundane purposes of making cookware non-stick and fabric waterproof. That they function, incidentally, as pervasive thyroid disruptors at the population level is not the result of malice. It is the result of the chemical industry's consistent practice of deploying substances at scale before understanding their biological effects, and regulators' consistent practice of requiring proof of harm so conclusive that by the time it arrives, the damage has been done to generations.

PFAS are not the only chemical class conducting this assault. They may not even be the worst. But they are the most extensively studied, the most universally distributed, and the most illustrative of how thoroughly modern industrial chemistry has infiltrated the endocrine system's most vulnerable organ.

## PFAS: The Mechanism at the Molecular Level

PFAS — a class of over 12,000 individual compounds — achieved their commercial utility from the same property that makes them biologically dangerous: the carbon-fluorine bond, one of the strongest in organic chemistry. Conventional metabolic pathways cannot break it. Biological systems cannot excrete PFAS compounds efficiently. They persist, accumulate, and, at the concentrations that accumulation produces, interact with biological systems in ways that took decades to characterize.

The thyroid disruption mechanisms are now documented at multiple levels.

The first and best established is competitive displacement at thyroid hormone transport proteins. PFAS — particularly PFOS and PFOA, the longest-studied compounds in the class — bind to transthyretin (TTR) and thyroid-binding globulin (TBG), the proteins that carry T4 through the bloodstream. This competitive binding displaces T4 from its transport proteins, reduces the amount of bound thyroid hormone available for cellular delivery, and alters the free-to-bound ratio in ways that are not fully compensated by the feedback loop. The pituitary may not see this disruption clearly — creating, again, the pattern of normal-seeming TSH with functionally compromised thyroid hormone delivery.

The second mechanism, characterized in more detail by Michigan State University researchers in a 2024 ACS Omega study, operates at the synthesis level. Mid- to long-chain PFAS impact thyroid hormone synthesis by changing the local hydrogen bond network as well as the required orientation of hormonogenic residues in thyroglobulin, stopping the production of thyroxine (T4). The sulfonic PFAS compounds — PFOS and PFHxS — show more prominent toxic effects than the carboxylic variants. This is the first molecular-level demonstration of PFAS directly interfering with thyroid hormone synthesis itself, not merely its transport.

The third mechanism involves thyroid peroxidase inhibition. Several PFAS compounds have been shown to inhibit TPO activity — the same enzyme that Hashimoto's autoimmunity targets. PFAS may, in effect, be conducting a chemical Hashimoto's: impairing the enzyme responsible for the first step of thyroid hormone production without requiring an autoimmune response to do so.

The fourth mechanism is autoimmune activation. Chronic PFAS exposure has been associated with elevated thyroid peroxidase antibodies and thyroglobulin antibodies in epidemiological studies, suggesting that PFAS exposure may be a trigger for or amplifier of autoimmune thyroid disease — working through the immune dysregulation and oxidative stress pathways that PFAS generate in thyroid tissue.

## The Epidemiological Picture

The National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) has consistently found that individuals with higher blood PFAS levels have a significantly increased risk of hypothyroidism. Women with elevated PFAS concentrations show a roughly 36% greater likelihood of thyroid hormone disruption compared to those with lower exposure levels.

The C8 Health Project — a large epidemiological study of communities in Ohio and West Virginia near a DuPont PFOA-manufacturing facility — produced some of the most detailed human exposure data available. The populations around this facility had PFAS blood concentrations orders of magnitude higher than the general population due to drinking water contamination, and the thyroid disease rates in these communities were correspondingly elevated.

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis found that PFAS exposure may be a risk factor for thyroid cancer, with some studies showing significant positive associations between PFOA exposure and thyroid cancer incidence. The magnitude and consistency of this association across studies varies, but the direction is clear enough to have prompted the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai's Department of Otolaryngology to include PFAS exposure as a standard question in thyroid cancer patient intake.

The Michigan PFAS Exposure and Health Study, published in Scientific Reports in 2025, examined 728 adolescents and adults living in a PFAS-contaminated drinking water area. Analyzing 39 individual PFAS compounds against four thyroid hormone measures, researchers found that increasing PFAS concentrations — particularly PFUnA — were associated with decreasing total T3. The mixture analysis confirmed the directional finding: PFAS exposure, at the population level, is suppressing active thyroid hormone.

The transgenerational data are what make this situation especially grim. PFAS exposure during pregnancy generates alterations that may be passed to children, and in turn to grandchildren, through epigenetic changes that occur in the parental germline. The 2024 review published in Endocrinology, Diabetes & Metabolism characterizes this multi-generational transmission: direct prenatal PFAS exposure during fetal development generates epigenetic marks — DNA methylation changes at thyroid-relevant gene promoters — that alter thyroid axis function in the offspring, independently of whether the child is directly exposed. This is not a hypothetical. It has been demonstrated in animal models, and the epidemiological evidence in human birth cohorts is accumulating.

The implication is that the PFAS-thyroid damage already accumulated in the population is not bounded by current exposure levels. Even if all PFAS production ceased today — which it has not — the epigenetic legacy of past exposure would continue to shape thyroid function in the children and grandchildren of exposed populations.

## Fluoride: The Contentious One

Fluoride's thyroid effects represent the most politically charged debate in this area, partly because water fluoridation is a public health policy with genuine historical benefits in dental health, and partly because the anti-fluoridation movement has been colonized by general conspiracy thinking to the point where serious scientific concerns about fluoride's thyroid effects have become associated with the less credible end of the argument.

The chemistry is unambiguous and has been understood for decades: fluoride is a competitive inhibitor of the sodium-iodide symporter (NIS) — the protein responsible for concentrating iodine in the thyroid gland. Iodine and fluoride share similar ionic radii and charge; fluoride can displace iodide at the NIS. At high concentrations, fluoride also inhibits thyroid peroxidase.

The clinical question is whether fluoride at the concentrations present in fluoridated drinking water — typically 0.7 parts per million in the United States — is sufficient to cause thyroid dysfunction at the population level.

Several lines of evidence suggest it may be, particularly in iodine-deficient individuals. A 2018 UK study published in the Journal of Epidemiology & Community Health, examining over 97,000 patients in 207 general practices, found that practices in fluoridated areas were nearly twice as likely to have high rates of hypothyroidism compared to practices in non-fluoridated areas, after adjusting for confounders. A follow-up analysis specifically found elevated TSH levels in fluoridated communities.

The National Toxicology Program (NTP) conducted a meta-analysis of fluoride and cognitive effects published in 2024, finding moderate-confidence evidence that fluoride is associated with lower IQ in children at levels above 1.5 mg/L — higher than US fluoridation standards but within the range of naturally occurring fluoride in groundwater in many regions. The cognitive pathway appears to run, at least in part, through thyroid disruption: thyroid hormone is essential for fetal and neonatal brain development, and fluoride's competitive inhibition of iodine uptake in a marginally iodine-deficient population may be enough to impair fetal thyroid status at a critical developmental window.

The mainstream public health community has largely resisted incorporating fluoride thyroid concerns into policy discussions, arguing that the studies showing effects at 0.7 mg/L are confounded and methodologically limited. The argument has merit. But the consistent directional finding across diverse study populations — and the plausible biochemical mechanism involving competitive NIS inhibition — meets the threshold for precautionary concern that should, at minimum, trigger more adequately funded research. The fact that the largest recent independent risk assessment (the NTP) concluded there is sufficient evidence to support a hazard conclusion for fluoride at high doses is not a finding that should be dismissed by citing its limitations.

## Perchlorate: The Invisible Competitor

Perchlorate is a naturally occurring and industrial compound — found in rocket fuel, explosives, fertilizers, and, due to natural mineralization processes, in significant concentrations in groundwater across much of the American Southwest and in imported produce. It is a potent, well-characterized inhibitor of the sodium-iodide symporter, competing with iodide for the same transport protein.

The Centers for Disease Control's NHANES data have consistently detected perchlorate in the urine of virtually all Americans tested — it is nearly universal exposure. The Environmental Protection Agency has oscillated for decades on whether to set a maximum contaminant level (MCL) for perchlorate in drinking water. As of 2023, the EPA finalized a maximum contaminant level of 0.056 mg/L for perchlorate — the first federal drinking water standard. Whether this limit is sufficiently protective for pregnant women and iodine-deficient populations is disputed.

The interaction between perchlorate and iodine status is important and often overlooked. In iodine-sufficient individuals with robust NIS activity, competitive inhibition by perchlorate at low concentrations may be compensated by increased NIS expression or increased dietary iodine. In iodine-deficient individuals — a category, as we will examine in Part 4, that includes a substantial fraction of American pregnant women — there is less reserve to compensate, and the thyroid disruption from perchlorate exposure may be clinically significant even at low levels.

## BPA, Phthalates, and the Broader Endocrine Disruption Landscape

Bisphenol A (BPA) is an industrial chemical used in polycarbonate plastics and epoxy resins that line food cans. It is a well-established endocrine disruptor. Its thyroid-specific effects involve multiple mechanisms: BPA can bind to thyroid hormone receptors, acting as either an agonist or antagonist depending on context; it can inhibit T4 binding to transport proteins; and it can interfere with thyroid hormone metabolism.

A systematic review of human studies found consistent inverse associations between urinary BPA concentrations and thyroid hormone levels, particularly T4, in pregnant women. The same study populations frequently show elevated TSH, consistent with a hypothyroid direction of effect. BPA exposure is particularly concerning during pregnancy, both because the developing fetus is exquisitely sensitive to thyroid hormone levels and because BPA readily crosses the placenta.

Phthalates — plasticizers found in PVC products, personal care products, food packaging, and pharmaceutical coatings — have documented anti-thyroid effects through multiple pathways including competitive binding to transport proteins and inhibition of deiodinase activity. Several phthalate metabolites have been associated with reduced T4 and T3 levels in epidemiological studies of pregnant women. Like PFAS, phthalates are near-universal in human exposure.

The challenge is not identifying individual chemicals with thyroid-disrupting effects. The challenge is characterizing the mixture toxicology: humans are not exposed to one thyroid disruptor at a time but to simultaneous low-level exposures to PFAS, perchlorate, fluoride, BPA, phthalates, pesticides, and heavy metals, all of which interact through partially overlapping pathways. The "safe" level of any one of these compounds, established through single-substance dose-response studies, may be a fiction in the real-world context of simultaneous multi-compound exposure. Regulatory frameworks were not designed for mixture toxicology, and they have not been reformed to address it.

## Pesticides: The Agricultural Contribution

The organophosphate pesticide chlorpyrifos — widely used in American agriculture until the EPA banned its use on food crops in 2021, following nearly two decades of contested regulatory proceedings — is a documented thyroid disruptor. It inhibits thyroid peroxidase activity and has been associated with both elevated TPO antibodies and altered thyroid hormone levels in exposed populations. Its cognitive effects appear to operate, at least in part, through the thyroid pathway: prenatal chlorpyrifos exposure disrupts maternal and fetal thyroid hormone levels at critical neurodevelopmental windows.

The organochlorine pesticides — compounds like DDT and its metabolite DDE, dioxins, and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) — were banned in most developed countries decades ago but persist in the environment and in human tissue due to their extreme chemical stability. They continue to show up in thyroid tissue samples, umbilical cord blood, and breast milk. PCBs competitively bind to thyroid hormone transport proteins and can structurally mimic thyroid hormones, binding to thyroid hormone receptors and altering gene expression. Even at the low concentrations present in the general population today, these legacy compounds may be contributing to subclinical thyroid disruption across age cohorts.

## The Regulatory Failure

The pattern in all of this is not random. It is consistent with a regulatory framework that has been systematically inadequate to the pace and scale of industrial chemical production.

The Toxic Substances Control Act, which governs chemical safety in the United States, was enacted in 1976 and allowed approximately 62,000 chemicals already in commerce to be grandfathered in without safety testing. The Frank R. Lautenberg Chemical Safety for the 21st Century Act, which reformed TSCA in 2016, improved the framework but retained a presumption of industrial utility and placed the burden of risk demonstration on the EPA rather than on manufacturers. The result is a system where chemicals can be in widespread commercial use — entering drinking water, food packaging, consumer products, and human bodies — for decades before sufficient evidence accumulates for regulatory action, and where, when action is finally taken (as with PFAS), the compounds have already distributed into soil, water, wildlife, and human tissue in ways that no regulatory limit can undo.

The thyroid specifically has paid a disproportionate price for this framework. It is the organ most sensitive to iodine competition, most responsive to oxidative stress, most susceptible to transport protein displacement, and most consequential in its dysfunction for neurological development. It is, in a sense, the canary in the coal mine for industrial chemical exposure — and the canary has been singing for decades.

The response has been, to put it charitably, measured.

## What People Can Do

This is the section that, in a lesser publication, would consist of actionable tips styled as empowerment. The honest version is more restrained.

Reducing PFAS exposure involves filtering drinking water through reverse osmosis or activated carbon systems, avoiding non-stick cookware with PTFE coatings, and minimizing consumption of produce known to be grown in PFAS-contaminated soil — a geographic lottery that most people have no practical way to navigate. It involves checking whether one's drinking water source has been contaminated, a process that requires public utility data and map literacy that the majority of the population does not have immediate access to.

Reducing perchlorate and nitrate exposure involves filtering drinking water and being aware of produce sourced from the American Southwest and from regions using Chilean nitrate fertilizers.

Reducing BPA and phthalate exposure involves choosing glass, stainless steel, or BPA-free containers — though some BPA-free products have substituted BPS and BPF, chemicals with similar endocrine-disrupting profiles — avoiding canned food where possible, and being aware that "fragrance" in personal care products frequently contains phthalate compounds.

None of this is comprehensive. All of it is harder for low-income households. And none of it addresses the background PFAS, perchlorate, and organochlorine contamination that is now essentially irreducible in the standard American food supply and water system.

The appropriate response to this situation is primarily political, not personal. It requires the regulatory overhaul of chemical safety assessment, the funding of mixture toxicology research, the mandatory testing of new chemicals before commercial deployment, and the serious financial liability of industries that have contaminated shared water supplies with chemicals known to impair thyroid function.

Whether any of that happens at the scale required is not a scientific question. It is a question about whether democratic societies can be organized to protect their endocrine systems from the externalities of an unregulated chemical economy. The answer, historically, has not been encouraging.
