# THE THYROID SERIES — Part 4
# The Fetal Blueprint
### *Thyroid hormones, the developing brain, the iodine deficiency hiding in plain sight, and the screening failure that is costing children their cognitive futures*

---

The human brain does not build itself. It is built, in the first trimester of pregnancy, according to an orchestrated molecular program whose execution depends critically on the availability of thyroid hormone — supplied, at that stage, entirely by the mother, because the fetal thyroid gland does not become functional until approximately weeks 18–20 of gestation. For the first half of pregnancy, the fetus's brain is developing on the mother's thyroid alone.

This is not a minor dependency. Thyroid hormones govern neuronal proliferation, migration, differentiation, and myelination during fetal development. They determine the architecture of the cerebral cortex, the connectivity of the hippocampus, and the development of the auditory and visual systems. Even a transient period of maternal thyroid hormone insufficiency during a critical developmental window can produce irreversible changes to fetal brain structure.

The severe end of this failure is familiar: cretinism, the profound intellectual disability and physical developmental abnormality resulting from severe maternal iodine deficiency, was endemic in iodine-poor mountain regions worldwide until salt iodization programs in the twentieth century addressed the most extreme cases. Less familiar — and arguably more consequential in the developed world today — is the subclinical end: the partial, undetected, inadequately corrected thyroid dysfunction occurring in pregnant women who test within the clinical "normal range" but whose hormone supply is insufficient for optimal fetal neurodevelopment.

This is a massive, ongoing, preventable public health problem. It is not being addressed with anything approaching proportionate urgency.

---

## What the Fetal Brain Needs, and When

The neurodevelopmental dependency on thyroid hormone is not uniform across gestation. It follows a highly specific temporal pattern that makes the first trimester — before the fetal thyroid is functional — the period of greatest vulnerability.

Weeks 4–6: Neural tube closure and early brain vesicle formation. Thyroid hormone signaling is active in neural tissue from the earliest stages. T3 regulates the expression of genes controlling neuronal differentiation in the developing cortex.

Weeks 8–20: Neuronal proliferation in the germinal matrix and migration to cortical layers. This process generates billions of neurons that must migrate from their birthplace to their final cortical destinations along radial glial scaffolds — a process directly regulated by thyroid hormone. Disruption produces heterotopic neurons: cells that end up in the wrong cortical layer, forming the structural substrate for the cognitive and learning deficits seen in children of hypothyroid mothers.

Weeks 20 onwards: The fetal thyroid begins to function, but the maternal thyroid continues to contribute significantly throughout pregnancy. Myelination — the insulation of axonal projections that determines the speed and efficiency of neural transmission — continues into early childhood and is thyroid hormone-dependent throughout.

The auditory system provides a particularly sensitive biomarker. Congenital deafness and auditory processing disorders are strongly associated with both congenital hypothyroidism and maternal hypothyroidism during pregnancy. The cochlea and auditory brainstem develop with precise thyroid hormone dependency; timing of exposure loss by even one to two weeks can determine whether specific auditory structures form correctly.

## The Iodine Deficiency That Wasn't Supposed to Happen Here

Iodine deficiency was supposed to have been solved. The introduction of iodized salt in the 1920s is credited with eliminating endemic goiter and cretinism from most of the developed world. For decades, public health agencies assumed the problem was resolved in wealthy nations and confined to developing countries.

The assumption was wrong.

NHANES data collected from 2011 to 2020 showed declining median urinary iodine concentrations (UIC) among women of reproductive age in the United States. More significantly, urinary iodine concentrations in pregnant women were consistently below the WHO-recommended threshold of 150 μg/L for pregnant women. Depending on the study methodology and population examined, between 23% and 59% of American pregnant women show inadequate iodine status.

A 2025 review in Nutrition Reviews documented this resurgence in detail, identifying the primary contributing factor: reduced milk consumption. Dairy products are the primary dietary source of iodine in the United States, because cows are fed iodine-supplemented feed and because iodophor disinfectants used in dairy processing contribute to milk iodine content. The decline in milk consumption driven by dietary trends, lactose intolerance awareness, and plant-based milk alternatives has removed the primary dietary iodine source for millions of women without an equivalent replacement. Plant-based milks — oat, almond, soy, coconut — contain essentially no iodine unless specifically fortified.

The United Kingdom has no national salt iodization program. UK pregnant women show a median UIC of approximately 85 μg/L — well below the WHO threshold. Denmark, despite widespread use of iodine-containing supplements after the introduction of salt iodization, still shows median UICs of 68 μg/L in pregnant women who do not supplement. Belgium shows a median of 124 μg/L, below the threshold despite high supplementation rates.

The global burden data from the Global Burden of Disease 2021 study confirm what national surveys suggest: iodine deficiency in women of reproductive age has declined globally from 1990 to 2019, but high-income regions, including the United States, now face a resurgence specifically among pregnant women — the exact population where iodine deficiency has the most severe consequences.

## The IQ Equation

The cognitive consequences of mild-to-moderate maternal iodine deficiency are measured, real, and irreversible in affected children.

The Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents and Children (ALSPAC) — one of the most comprehensive birth cohort studies ever conducted — found that children of mothers with mild iodine deficiency in early pregnancy (defined as a UIC below 150 μg/L) showed significantly lower IQ scores at age 8 and poorer reading ability at age 9 compared to children of iodine-sufficient mothers. The effect size was not enormous — the IQ difference was approximately 3 points in the mildly deficient group — but it was statistically robust, present after adjustment for multiple confounders, and population-attributable fraction calculations suggested it was affecting a substantial fraction of the study's children.

Three IQ points, on first consideration, does not sound alarming. At the population level, it is staggering. The ALSPAC study population represented a developed-country birth cohort. If a 3-point IQ shift is occurring in UK children of mildly iodine-deficient mothers, and if 23–59% of American pregnant women have inadequate iodine status, the aggregate cognitive impact is not a rounding error in population intelligence — it is a measurable suppression of cognitive capacity in the next generation.

A 2025 review in Nutrients confirmed a U-shaped relationship: both insufficient and excessive iodine exposure during pregnancy may impair optimal brain development. Excessive iodine — particularly in women with underlying thyroid autoimmunity, in whom high iodine intake can trigger or worsen autoimmune hypothyroidism — carries its own risks. The therapeutic window is real: the recommendation for pregnant women is 220–250 μg/day, which should ideally come from a combination of iodized salt and a prenatal supplement containing at least 150 μg of iodine. The American Thyroid Association recommends a prenatal vitamin containing 150 μg of iodide (not iodate, and not from kelp, whose iodine content is variable and sometimes dangerously excessive).

The fact that many popular prenatal vitamins do not contain any iodine, and that obstetric providers rarely discuss iodine supplementation with pregnant patients, represents a meaningful failure in prenatal care.

## Maternal Hypothyroidism and the Autism Signal

Part 1's overview article first raised the thyroid-autism connection. Part 4 is where it deserves the full examination it has earned.

A study published in November 2025 in The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism by researchers at Ben-Gurion University of the Negev tracked a retrospective Israeli cohort of mothers and offspring. The findings: children of mothers with hypothyroidism that persisted across multiple trimesters showed more than a twofold increased risk of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) diagnosis, compared to children of euthyroid mothers. Crucially, the elevated risk was present specifically in mothers with persistent, inadequately controlled hypothyroidism — not in mothers whose hypothyroidism was treated and controlled. Adequately treated hypothyroidism was not associated with elevated ASD risk.

This is a highly specific finding. It says: the thyroid disease is not the variable. The uncontrolled thyroid hormone deficiency is the variable. This distinction points toward a causal mechanism — thyroid hormone insufficiency at critical neurodevelopmental windows — rather than a shared genetic or confounded association. And it carries a direct clinical implication: identifying and adequately treating maternal hypothyroidism during pregnancy would, according to this model, reduce the risk of ASD in offspring.

The PFAS pathway connects here. The MARBLES birth cohort study at UC Davis, examining 151 pregnant women who later had children with ASD, typical development, or non-typical development, found that prenatal PFAS exposures differentially affected maternal thyroid function based on child neurodevelopmental outcomes. The thyroid disruption mechanism is not a coincidence in this context. PFAS disrupts maternal thyroid function. Disrupted maternal thyroid function impairs fetal neurodevelopment. Impaired fetal neurodevelopment elevates ASD risk.

This is a three-link chain, each link of which has independent evidential support. Whether PFAS-mediated thyroid disruption is a meaningful contributor to rising ASD rates — alongside the genetic, environmental, and diagnostic expansion factors that are clearly operating — is not yet formally established in causal terms. But the convergence of three independent lines of evidence around the same pathway is sufficiently strong that dismissing it requires a positive argument, not just uncertainty.

Maternal thyroid autoimmunity — the presence of TPO antibodies — adds another dimension. A Finnish case-control study found that maternal TPO antibody positivity during pregnancy was associated with an odds ratio of 1.78 for ASD diagnosis in offspring. Women with TPO-Ab positivity show blunted thyroidal response to human chorionic gonadotropin in early gestation, resulting in lower free T4 and higher TSH compared to antibody-negative women. Thyroid autoimmunity may be contributing to ASD risk through the same pathway of functional thyroid hormone insufficiency at critical developmental windows, without the mother necessarily meeting clinical criteria for hypothyroidism.

## Thyroid Hormone in Miscarriage and Preterm Birth

The pregnancy consequences of thyroid dysfunction extend well beyond the ASD question to miscarriage, preterm birth, and obstetric complications.

The 2024 ASRM guideline on subclinical hypothyroidism in the infertile female population notes that inadequate treatment of subclinical hypothyroidism can lead to infertility, miscarriage, and adverse obstetrical and neurodevelopmental outcomes. The mechanism involves thyroid hormone's role in endometrial receptivity and implantation biology: T3 receptors are expressed in the endometrium and decidua, and their activation is required for the molecular changes that enable embryo implantation and placentation.

Preeclampsia, placental abruption, and preterm delivery are significantly more common in women with untreated subclinical hypothyroidism during the first trimester. The risks are most elevated when SCH is defined at the lower TSH threshold of 2.5 mIU/L — the threshold recommended for pregnant women by the American Thyroid Association specifically because the normal physiological TSH range shifts lower during pregnancy under the influence of hCG stimulation of the thyroid.

The implication is that a woman who enters pregnancy with a TSH of 3.5 — technically within the "normal" range for the general population — may be functionally subclinical hypothyroid for pregnancy purposes, and that her obstetric outcomes and her child's neurodevelopmental trajectory may be adversely affected by that functional deficit.

The TSH debate from Part 1 lands differently in the prenatal context. The stakes are not just individual quality of life but fetal brain development and pregnancy viability. The case for earlier intervention, more sensitive thresholds, and routine first-trimester thyroid screening is stronger here than anywhere else in thyroid medicine.

## The Screening Failure

The American Thyroid Association recommends testing TSH levels in women who are actively trying to conceive if they have symptoms of thyroid disease, known risk factors, or a history of prior thyroid disease. This is not universal screening.

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) has historically recommended against universal thyroid screening in pregnancy, arguing that the evidence base for treating subclinical hypothyroidism in pregnant women without risk factors is insufficient to justify the cost and medicalization of routine screening.

Both organizations are operating from the same evidence base and reaching different policy conclusions, which is itself instructive. The evidence that treating overt hypothyroidism in pregnancy improves outcomes is unambiguous. The evidence that treating subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH 2.5–4.0) in antibody-negative women without symptoms improves outcomes is less clear — randomized controlled trials have not consistently shown benefit, and one large RCT (the CATS trial) showed no significant improvement in child IQ at age 3 when levothyroxine treatment was started in the second trimester.

The CATS trial's limitations, however, are material to this interpretation. Treatment began in the second trimester — after the window of maximum fetal thyroid hormone dependency, which is concentrated in the first trimester. Starting treatment after the horse has left the barn is not a valid test of whether earlier treatment would have been beneficial. The timing of intervention is central to whether it works, and the trials that fail to show benefit by intervening late are not evidence that early intervention fails.

The November 2025 Israeli ASD study, the iodine deficiency resurgence data, the PFAS-thyroid-autism pathway, and the mechanistic literature on first-trimester thyroid hormone dependency all point toward a clinical policy that the guidelines have not adopted: universal first-trimester thyroid screening with TSH, free T4, and TPO antibodies for all pregnant women, combined with proactive iodine supplementation counseling.

The cost of this screening is trivial. The cost of not doing it — measured in miscarriages, preterm births, compromised neurodevelopment, and preventable autism risk — is not trivial. It is simply borne by children who have no political voice, whose developmental deficits may not become apparent until years after the window for prevention has closed.

## The Sleep-Deprivation Multiplier

A specifically modern pregnancy risk factor deserves acknowledgment: the intersection of sleep deprivation with thyroid vulnerability during gestation. Pregnant women are, physiologically and socially, among the most sleep-deprived demographic in Western societies. First-trimester insomnia, third-trimester physical discomfort, and the cultural expectation that prenatal discomfort is normal and to be endured all contribute to chronic sleep insufficiency in pregnant women.

Sleep deprivation, as established in the broader research literature, suppresses regulatory T-cell activity, increases pro-inflammatory cytokine production, and activates the HPA stress axis — all of which feed into the autoimmune activation and cortisol-mediated thyroid hormone suppression pathways described in Parts 1 and 2. In a pregnant woman who is marginally iodine-deficient, carrying a PFAS body burden from decades of prior exposure, with a gut microbiome reflecting years of antibiotic use and low-fiber diet, and sleeping six hours per night — the aggregate thyroid risk is not additive. It is potentially multiplicative. The entire system that is supposed to protect fetal neurodevelopment is under simultaneous stress from multiple directions.

This is not alarmism. It is physiology. And it argues for a pregnancy care model that takes thyroid function, nutritional status, sleep, and environmental exposure as co-equal clinical priorities — not as peripheral concerns to be addressed, if at all, by a specialty referral after something goes demonstrably wrong.
