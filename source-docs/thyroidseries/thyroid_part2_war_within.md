# THE THYROID SERIES — Part 2
# The War Within
### *Hashimoto's, Graves', and the gut that may be starting the fight*

---

Every year, Hashimoto's thyroiditis is diagnosed in roughly 14 million Americans. Globally, it is the most common autoimmune disease in the world, affecting an estimated 5% of the population — with the number rising in virtually every country where it is tracked. In women of reproductive age, the prevalence approaches 10%. Nobody has fully explained why it keeps rising. The standard answer — genetic predisposition plus environmental trigger — is technically accurate and operationally empty. It tells you almost nothing useful.

What the research published in the last two to three years tells you is considerably more. The origin story of autoimmune thyroid disease is being rewritten, and the protagonist is not where most people expected to find it. It is in the gut.

---

## What Hashimoto's and Graves' Disease Actually Are

Before examining the new mechanisms, the pathology itself deserves a clear-eyed account, because both conditions are frequently described in terms that flatten their complexity.

Hashimoto's thyroiditis is a chronic autoimmune condition in which the immune system mounts a sustained, destructive attack on the thyroid gland. The primary weapons are two autoantibodies: anti-thyroid peroxidase antibodies (anti-TPO) and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies (anti-TG). Thyroid peroxidase is the enzyme responsible for the iodination of thyroglobulin — the first step in thyroid hormone synthesis. Thyroglobulin is the protein scaffold on which T4 and T3 are assembled. Antibodies against both of these molecules represent an immune system that has lost tolerance to the machinery of thyroid hormone production. The result is lymphocytic infiltration of the thyroid tissue, progressive follicular destruction, fibrosis, and ultimately glandular failure. Most patients end up hypothyroid, though they may cycle through a hyperthyroid phase early in the disease — sometimes called Hashitoxicosis — as damaged follicles release stored hormone into circulation.

Graves' disease is the mirror image in mechanism and the opposite in clinical effect. The driving antibodies in Graves' — thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSIs) — do not destroy thyroid cells. They mimic TSH. They bind to the TSH receptor and activate it constitutively, telling the thyroid to produce hormones whether the pituitary signals for it or not. The result is persistent hyperthyroidism: accelerated metabolism, weight loss, heat intolerance, tremor, palpitations, insomnia, and, in the roughly 40% of Graves' patients who develop thyroid eye disease (TED), the orbital inflammation and proptosis that can disfigure and blind.

Both diseases share an autoimmune architecture: genetic susceptibility, primarily involving HLA-DR alleles and other immune-regulatory gene variants, combined with environmental triggers that break immune tolerance to thyroid-specific antigens. The mystery has always been what those triggers are and why they are becoming more common.

The gut axis is now the most compelling candidate for at least one major mechanistic pathway.

---

## The Gut as Immunological Headquarters

The gut contains approximately 70–80% of the body's immune cells. This is not incidental. The intestinal surface is the largest interface between the internal environment of the body and the microbially colonized exterior. It must simultaneously allow the absorption of nutrients and the sampling of the microbial world — while preventing the systemic invasion of that world's contents. The tight junctions between intestinal epithelial cells are the operational boundary of this balance.

When those tight junctions are compromised — a state colloquially known as "leaky gut" and more precisely termed increased intestinal permeability — the balance shifts. Bacterial antigens, lipopolysaccharides (LPS), and partially digested food proteins that would normally remain in the intestinal lumen gain access to the subepithelial immune tissue. There, they encounter the densest concentration of immune surveillance in the body.

A 2021 study measuring serum zonulin — a biomarker of intestinal tight junction integrity — in Hashimoto's patients found significantly elevated levels compared to healthy controls, with concentrations correlating positively with interferon-gamma (an inflammatory cytokine) levels. The interpretation: patients with Hashimoto's thyroiditis show measurable evidence of increased intestinal permeability, and that permeability correlates with systemic immune activation.

This is not, by itself, proof that leaky gut causes Hashimoto's. The relationship could run in the opposite direction — thyroid dysfunction is known to slow gut motility and alter gut ecology, potentially causing the permeability. But the evidence increasingly supports bidirectionality: a vicious cycle in which gut dysbiosis promotes immune activation, immune activation damages the thyroid, thyroid dysfunction worsens gut motility, and worsened gut motility deepens dysbiosis.

The gut-thyroid connection is further grounded in a striking embryological fact: the thyroid and the intestinal epithelium share common developmental origins. Both derive from the endoderm during fetal development. Gut and thyroid follicular cells exhibit structural and functional parallels that may facilitate the reciprocal influences between them. They are, in a sense, distant relatives — which may explain why the gut's immune surveillance system can mistake one for the other.

## Molecular Mimicry: The Case of Mistaken Identity

The specific mechanism by which gut dysbiosis triggers thyroid autoimmunity involves one of the most conceptually elegant — and clinically alarming — processes in immunology: molecular mimicry.

The principle is this: if a foreign antigen (a bacterial protein, for example) is structurally similar to a self-antigen (a human protein), the immune response raised against the foreign invader can cross-react with the body's own tissue. The immune system is not making an error, precisely; it is doing its job with imprecise tools.

For thyroid autoimmunity, the relevant mimicry has been identified with considerable molecular specificity. Lactobacilli and Bifidobacteria — bacteria present in the healthy gut — have protein sequences that share structural similarity with thyroid peroxidase and thyroglobulin. When gut dysbiosis destabilizes the intestinal barrier, allowing abnormally large quantities of bacterial antigens to reach subepithelial immune tissue, the immune system can mount a response that then cross-reacts with the structurally similar thyroid proteins.

A 2024 study in Science Advances used Mendelian randomization — a technique that leverages genetic variation to approximate causal inference — to demonstrate a significant association between Helicobacter pylori infection and autoimmune thyroid diseases. H. pylori is the bacterium responsible for most peptic ulcers; it is also one of the most prevalent chronic bacterial infections in the world. The molecular mimicry hypothesis suggests that H. pylori antigens resemble thyroid antigens closely enough to initiate cross-reactive immune responses in genetically susceptible individuals.

The oral microbiome adds a further layer. Oral dysbiosis — shifts in bacterial populations in the mouth — has been linked to autoimmune thyroid diseases through similar molecular mimicry mechanisms. Oral bacteria can enter the bloodstream through inflamed gingival tissue, reach the systemic circulation, and trigger immune responses that may cross-react with thyroid antigens. Patients with Hashimoto's thyroiditis show elevated prevalence and greater severity of periodontal disease symptoms. Whether the periodontitis causes or reflects the systemic autoimmune state is not fully resolved, but the association is consistent enough to suggest clinical relevance.

## The Microbial Signatures of Disease

16S rRNA metagenomic sequencing — which allows comprehensive profiling of bacterial populations from stool samples — has produced a detailed picture of gut microbial differences between people with autoimmune thyroid disease and healthy controls.

The data are consistent across multiple studies, though not perfectly uniform. Patients with Hashimoto's thyroiditis and Graves' disease show reduced microbial diversity compared to healthy individuals. Specific genera appear repeatedly: elevated Lactobacillus, Veillonella, and Streptococcus in Graves' disease patients; reduced Blautia and butyrate-producing bacteria across autoimmune thyroid conditions; and reduced Akkermansia muciniphila — a keystone species responsible for maintaining mucus layer integrity and gut barrier function — in both conditions.

The loss of butyrate-producing bacteria is particularly significant. Butyrate, a short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) produced by bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber, is the primary energy source for intestinal epithelial cells. It directly maintains tight junction integrity, upregulates regulatory T-cell (Treg) differentiation, and suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine production. A reduction in butyrate-producing genera is not merely a marker of dysbiosis — it is a mechanistic driver of the increased intestinal permeability and immune dysregulation that enable molecular mimicry to occur.

The LPS-mediated pathway runs in parallel. When gram-negative bacteria die, they release lipopolysaccharide — a potent activator of the innate immune system via the TLR4 receptor. In a dysbiotic gut with increased permeability, LPS translocates into systemic circulation and triggers the downstream inflammatory signaling cascades — NF-κB activation, pro-inflammatory cytokine release — that promote autoimmune reactivity. Mouse models receiving fecal transplants from hypothyroid patients show reduced tight junction mRNA in the colon and increased serum LPS, confirming that the dysbiotic state itself is transferable.

## The Selenium Imperative

No nutritional intervention in thyroid autoimmunity is more consistently supported by the evidence than selenium supplementation in Hashimoto's thyroiditis.

Selenium is a critical cofactor for the thyroidal selenoproteins: the iodothyronine deiodinases (the conversion enzymes discussed in Part 1), the glutathione peroxidases (which protect thyroid cells from hydrogen peroxide generated during thyroid hormone synthesis), and the thioredoxin reductases (which maintain the redox state of thyroid cells). The thyroid gland has the highest selenium concentration per gram of tissue of any organ in the body — a measure of how metabolically dependent it is on the mineral.

A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis published in Thyroid, examining all randomized clinical trials of selenium supplementation in Hashimoto's patients, found that selenium supplementation significantly reduced thyroid antibody levels — both anti-TPO and anti-TG — independent of the patient's baseline selenium status and independent of concurrent thyroid hormone replacement. TSH levels also reduced with supplementation. The certainty of this evidence was characterized as moderate.

This is clinically significant in a way that warrants emphasis: selenium supplementation directly reduces the autoimmune attack on the thyroid gland, not just its downstream hormonal consequences. It is operating at the level of the disease process, not the symptom. The mechanism likely involves both direct reduction of oxidative stress in thyroid tissue (reducing the hydrogen peroxide that would otherwise damage thyroid cells and release antigenic fragments that perpetuate the immune response) and immunomodulatory effects of selenoproteins on T-cell function.

The current recommended dietary allowance for selenium is 55 μg/day for adults, 60 μg/day for pregnant women. Supplementation studies in Hashimoto's have typically used 200 μg/day. The tolerable upper intake level is 400 μg/day. The gap between what people typically consume — often 40–70 μg/day in countries with selenium-poor soils — and what appears to be therapeutic is not enormous, but it is real.

Iron deficiency adds a compounding problem. Iron is required for thyroid peroxidase function — the same enzyme that autoimmunity attacks. Without adequate iron, TPO activity is impaired, thyroid hormone synthesis suffers, and anemia from iron deficiency produces fatigue that is clinically indistinguishable from hypothyroid fatigue. Coexisting iron deficiency anemia in hypothyroid patients is common, underdiagnosed, and frequently unremedied, because the fatigue is attributed to the thyroid condition without checking iron status.

Vitamin D rounds out the nutritional picture. Multiple studies have found that patients with autoimmune thyroid diseases have significantly lower serum 25-OH vitamin D levels than matched controls, and that vitamin D supplementation can modestly reduce TPO antibody titers. The mechanism involves vitamin D's role in regulating regulatory T-cell activity — the same Treg cells whose suppression of autoimmune responses appears to be compromised in Hashimoto's and Graves' disease.

## Gluten, Hashimoto's, and the Molecular Mimicry Question

The relationship between gluten and Hashimoto's is controversial in the clinical literature and passionately embraced in patient communities, so intellectual honesty requires holding both facts simultaneously.

The mechanism proposed is molecular mimicry: the gliadin protein in gluten shares structural sequences with thyroid peroxidase. In individuals with increased intestinal permeability — the same leaky gut that drives bacterial antigen translocation — gliadin peptides that normally remain in the gut lumen gain access to subepithelial immune tissue. The immune response against gliadin cross-reacts with TPO, potentially initiating or perpetuating Hashimoto's.

The clinical evidence base for this is weaker than the mechanistic hypothesis would suggest. There is an established association between celiac disease — frank autoimmune gluten intolerance — and autoimmune thyroid disease, with celiac patients showing two to three times the rate of thyroid autoimmunity compared to the general population. This is consistent with either shared genetic susceptibility to autoimmunity, shared increased intestinal permeability, or direct molecular mimicry — or all three simultaneously.

The evidence that non-celiac gluten sensitivity in Hashimoto's patients responds to gluten elimination is largely observational, anecdotal, and confounded by the dietary and lifestyle changes that typically accompany a gluten elimination protocol. Patient surveys — including a 2015 survey of over 2,200 Hashimoto's patients by thyroid pharmacist Dr. Izabella Wentz — report substantial symptomatic improvement with gluten elimination, but such surveys cannot distinguish the specific effect of gluten removal from the broader effect of dietary quality improvement.

The current mainstream position — that gluten elimination is warranted in Hashimoto's patients with confirmed celiac disease but not routinely recommended for those without it — is defensible on the evidence. It may also be overcautious for a subset of patients whose intestinal permeability is demonstrably elevated, whose symptoms are not controlled by standard management, and for whom a well-executed dietary trial has minimal risk. The evidence gap here is real, but it is a gap that favors patient-directed experimentation, not a gap that closes the door.

## Graves' Disease: The Autoimmune Overstimulation

Graves' disease is less prevalent than Hashimoto's but, in its acute and severe forms, more immediately dangerous. Thyroid storm — extreme hyperthyroidism — is a medical emergency with a mortality rate of 10–30% even with treatment. The condition's relationship to stress is not metaphorical; major psychological stress events, immune reconstitution after pregnancy, and post-infectious immune activation are all recognized precipitants of Graves' onset or relapse.

The microbiome data in Graves' disease parallels but does not duplicate the Hashimoto's picture. Graves' patients show reduced alpha diversity and distinctively different bacterial profiles — elevated Lactobacillus, Veillonella, and Streptococcus, with positive correlations between TSH receptor antibody (TRAb) levels and the relative abundance of Lactobacillus and Ruminococcus. The mechanistic link between these specific bacteria and the production of thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins is not fully established, but the consistency of the microbial signature across independent studies is accumulating to the point where calling it a coincidence requires more argument than calling it meaningful.

A 2021 randomized controlled trial found that the probiotic Bifidobacterium longum, administered alongside the anti-thyroid drug methimazole, significantly reduced TRAb levels and improved thyroid function in Graves' patients compared to methimazole alone — working through the gut-thyroid axis. This is a small, single-center trial and requires replication, but it represents the first controlled clinical evidence that microbiome-targeted intervention directly affects the autoimmune process in Graves' disease.

The batoclimab story from Part 1's overview is worth deeper examination here. Batoclimab is a monoclonal antibody that blocks the neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn), which recycles IgG antibodies — including TSIs — back into circulation. Normally, IgG antibodies bound to FcRn are protected from lysosomal degradation; block the receptor, and the antibodies are degraded, and serum levels fall. The Phase 2a results in 2024 showed significant TSI reduction in Graves' patients. The therapy is mechanistically upstream of everything currently available for Graves' — it does not merely suppress the symptoms of TSI-driven hyperthyroidism, it reduces the autoantibody burden directly. If Phase 3 results confirm the Phase 2a data, this is likely to be a genuinely paradigm-shifting treatment.

## The Virome and Mycobiome: The Underexplored Frontier

The research on gut microbiome and autoimmune thyroid disease has overwhelmingly focused on bacteria. But the gut also contains vast communities of viruses — predominantly bacteriophages that infect gut bacteria — and fungi. The virome and mycobiome have been recognized as critical but underexplored components of the human microbiome, potentially contributing to immune dysregulation and the pathogenesis of autoimmune thyroid diseases.

Several human endogenous retroviruses (HERVs) — remnants of ancient viral integrations in the human genome — have been found at elevated expression levels in the thyroid tissue of Hashimoto's and Graves' patients, suggesting that these ancient viral sequences may be reactivated by environmental triggers and contribute to the autoimmune process. The HERV-K10 protein has demonstrated thyroid-specific immune reactivity. This is not fringe virology; it is a plausible and formally studied mechanistic pathway that remains underexplored partly because the methodological tools to study the virome comprehensively have only recently become available.

## Sleep, Circadian Rhythm, and Thyroid Autoimmunity

The 2025 AACE Year in Review highlighted the interplay between sleep and thyroid function as a newly prioritized research area — and the data emerging are more clinically relevant than the typical "sleep is good for you" messaging suggests.

TSH secretion follows a strong circadian pattern, peaking in the early morning hours and reaching its nadir in late afternoon. This rhythm is entrained by the same circadian clock mechanisms that govern cortisol secretion and melatonin production. Chronic sleep disruption — whether from sleep apnea, shift work, or voluntary restriction — desynchronizes this rhythmic TSH secretion, producing patterns of elevated nocturnal TSH and blunted morning TSH that may alter thyroid stimulation over time.

More relevantly for autoimmune thyroid disease: sleep deprivation is a potent activator of inflammatory pathways and a suppressant of regulatory T-cell activity. The regulatory T-cell deficiency that characterizes Hashimoto's and Graves' disease — where Tregs fail to adequately suppress the autoreactive lymphocytes attacking the thyroid — is worsened by the same sleep disruption that is now endemic in modern societies. Whether sleep-related immune dysregulation is a meaningful contributor to the rising prevalence of autoimmune thyroid disease, or merely an exacerbating factor in already established disease, is not yet settled. The mechanistic logic for a contributory role is sound.

---

## The Takeaway: A Systemic Disease With a Systemic Solution

The conventional clinical management of Hashimoto's thyroiditis consists almost entirely of monitoring TSH and prescribing levothyroxine when it becomes necessary. For Graves' disease, it is anti-thyroid drugs, radioactive iodine, or surgery — none of which addresses the underlying autoimmune mechanism.

The research accumulated over the past several years now supports a substantially richer clinical picture: Hashimoto's is an autoimmune disease with identifiable microbial, nutritional, and intestinal permeability contributions. Managing it optimally requires attending to gut ecology, selenium status, iron status, vitamin D levels, sleep hygiene, and the chronic inflammatory inputs — dietary, chemical, psychological — that maintain the dysbiotic state enabling molecular mimicry to proceed.

This is not alternative medicine. It is the mechanistic logic of the most current published research applied to a disease that the standard clinical encounter addresses in a reductive, symptom-suppressing way.

Whether that changes practice quickly enough to matter for the tens of millions currently managing autoimmune thyroid disease with a TSH measurement and a levothyroxine prescription is a different question. The honest answer is: probably not quickly.
