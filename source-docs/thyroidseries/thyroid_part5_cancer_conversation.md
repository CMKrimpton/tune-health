# THE THYROID SERIES — Part 5
# The Cancer Conversation
### *Overdiagnosis, the molecular revolution, the new surgical calculus, and why thyroid cancer treatment was overdue for a reckoning*

---

Thyroid cancer presents a paradox that reveals, with uncommon clarity, how medicine can go wrong in two opposite directions simultaneously.

For the past three decades, thyroid cancer was on a trajectory that looked epidemiologically alarming. Incidence rates tripled in the United States between 1975 and 2015. Other countries with advanced imaging infrastructure showed similar or steeper curves. The natural interpretation — that something was causing more thyroid cancer — drove funding, raised anxiety, and produced a clinical response calibrated to a genuine epidemic.

The incidence curve was real. The epidemic, mostly, was not.

A series of analyses beginning around 2014 established what retrospective examination of the data had suggested for years: the vast majority of the rising thyroid cancer incidence was driven by the increasing use of neck ultrasound and CT imaging, which detected small papillary microcarcinomas — tumors under 10 mm — that had always existed in the population but had never been found because nobody had looked for them. Studies of autopsy series found occult papillary thyroid microcarcinomas in anywhere from 5% to 36% of individuals who died of unrelated causes, with the higher estimates from populations where thyroid tissue was systematically sectioned. These were clinically invisible cancers that had coexisted with their hosts, causing no harm and never destined to.

The machine of modern oncology found them, labeled them cancer, and treated them with surgery, radioactive iodine, lifelong monitoring, and the psychological weight of a cancer diagnosis. In doing so, it almost certainly caused net harm to a large fraction of the patients it diagnosed.

This is the overdiagnosis problem in thyroid cancer — still not fully resolved, still generating excess surgery in many clinical settings, and now partially addressed by a set of 2025 guidelines that represent the most significant recalibration of thyroid cancer management in a decade. But the overdiagnosis story should not obscure the other half of the picture: the advanced thyroid cancers that are genuinely dangerous, that were undertreated before targeted molecular therapies, and that are now being addressed with a precision that was simply unavailable twenty years ago.

Both stories need telling.

---

## The Spectrum: What Thyroid Cancer Actually Is

Thyroid cancer is not one disease. It is a family of diseases with radically different natural histories, molecular drivers, and prognoses. Understanding where the overdiagnosis problem lives requires understanding this spectrum.

**Papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC)** accounts for roughly 80–85% of all thyroid cancers. It is the most common endocrine malignancy. Most papillary thyroid cancers — particularly the microcarcinomas under 1 cm — are indolent: they grow slowly or not at all, rarely metastasize, and have a long-term survival rate of approximately 99% with appropriate treatment. Multifocal PTC, where multiple small foci are present, is common and does not consistently predict more aggressive behavior.

**Follicular thyroid carcinoma (FTC)** accounts for approximately 10–15% of cases. It is more likely to spread hematogenously — through blood — to distant sites including lung and bone, rather than via lymph nodes. Its prognosis is generally good but somewhat less favorable than PTC.

**Poorly differentiated thyroid carcinoma (PDTC)** and **anaplastic thyroid carcinoma (ATC)** together account for a small fraction of cases but carry dramatically different prognoses. PDTC has an intermediate aggressiveness. ATC is one of the most lethal malignancies in oncology: it is largely unresponsive to radioactive iodine, has a median survival measured in months, and has historically been managed palliatively. New molecular targeted therapies, particularly for BRAF-mutated anaplastic cancers, are beginning to change this, but slowly.

**Medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC)** arises from parafollicular C-cells rather than follicular cells, does not take up iodine, and in 25% of cases is associated with germline mutations in the RET proto-oncogene. Familial MTC allows for prophylactic thyroidectomy in RET mutation carriers — one of the most successful preventive surgical interventions in oncology.

The overdiagnosis problem is almost entirely confined to the low end of the papillary spectrum: microcarcinomas that would never cause symptoms or death. The genuine clinical danger lies at the other end — in poorly differentiated and anaplastic cancers, in RAI-refractory differentiated cancers, and in metastatic disease.

## The 2025 ATA Guidelines: A New Surgical Calculus

The 2025 American Thyroid Association Management Guidelines for Adult Patients with Differentiated Thyroid Cancer — downloaded over 200,000 times in the months following their August publication — represent the most significant formal recalibration of thyroid cancer management since the 2015 guidelines.

The central revision is in the extent of initial surgery. For years, the default for most thyroid cancer diagnoses — including small, low-risk papillary cancers — was total thyroidectomy: removal of the entire gland. The logic was to facilitate radioactive iodine ablation of any remaining tissue and to use thyroglobulin as a reliable surveillance marker. The consequences were lifelong thyroid hormone replacement, permanent hypoparathyroidism in a significant minority of patients (parathyroid glands are frequently damaged or inadvertently removed during total thyroidectomy), and vocal cord paralysis from recurrent laryngeal nerve damage.

The new guidelines formally incorporate a decade of evidence showing that for low-risk, small PTC, thyroid lobectomy — removal of only the affected lobe — achieves outcomes equivalent to total thyroidectomy in terms of disease-specific survival and recurrence rates. The recurrence rates after lobectomy for low-risk PTC are low enough, and the life-years lost from lobectomy patients dying of thyroid cancer are rare enough, that the morbidity of total thyroidectomy cannot be justified on benefit grounds.

The 2025 guidelines also expand risk stratification considerably, moving toward a more individualized framework that accounts for tumor size, nodal status, extrathyroidal extension, molecular markers, and patient factors. The goal is to identify specifically which patients benefit from additional treatment — including radioactive iodine — rather than applying a uniform escalation to everyone whose diagnosis exceeds a threshold.

## Active Surveillance: Learning to Wait

The most philosophically challenging shift in thyroid cancer management is active surveillance for papillary microcarcinomas — the direct clinical response to the overdiagnosis recognition.

The foundational data come from Japan. Researchers at Kuma Hospital and the Cancer Institute Hospital in Tokyo began prospective active surveillance protocols for papillary microcarcinomas in the 1990s and have published long-term outcome data showing that the vast majority of these small cancers do not grow or metastasize over 5–10 years of observation. The growth rate is approximately 5–10% of lesions enlarging by more than 3 mm over 10 years. The risk of lymph node metastasis in observed patients who never receive surgery is low and, when it occurs, remains easily treatable.

Active surveillance is not universal. Exclusion criteria include tumors adjacent to the recurrent laryngeal nerve or trachea, cases with suspicious lymph nodes at presentation, aggressive cytological features, and patient-specific factors including significant anxiety, poor follow-up compliance, and pregnancy (where active surveillance creates monitoring complications). But for appropriately selected patients — a low-risk tumor in a patient who understands the surveillance requirements — active surveillance avoids surgery, its complications, and the lifelong pharmacological management of post-thyroidectomy hypothyroidism.

The North American evidence base for active surveillance has been slower to accumulate than the Japanese data, partly because the medicolegal culture in the United States creates institutional reluctance to recommend observation for anything labeled "cancer." Multiple North American centers have now published their active surveillance cohort data, and the outcomes are consistent with the Japanese experience. The 2025 ATA guidelines explicitly include active surveillance as an option for low-risk microcarcinomas.

The cultural barrier remains real. Telling a patient they have cancer and then recommending they do nothing requires a calibration of risk communication that most clinicians were not trained for. The word "cancer" triggers a categorical imperative in most people. Reframing small papillary thyroid lesions as "low-risk thyroid neoplasms of follicular origin" — a nomenclature change the WHO has actually adopted for the lowest-risk encapsulated non-invasive follicular variant PTC — is one approach to managing this problem. It resets the psychological stakes while accurately representing the biology.

## Thermal Ablation: Burning Without Cutting

For papillary microcarcinomas where the patient prefers intervention over surveillance but wants to avoid surgery, radiofrequency ablation (RFA) and microwave ablation (MWA) have matured into clinically validated alternatives.

These techniques use image-guided needle electrodes to thermally destroy tumor tissue in situ, with real-time ultrasound monitoring. They are performed under local anesthesia as outpatient procedures. They do not require general anesthesia. They preserve the thyroid gland and the contralateral lobe. They avoid the risks of open neck surgery — parathyroid damage, recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, infection, and scarring.

A 10-center study published in Radiology in 2024, comparing microwave ablation to surgical resection for multifocal T1N0M0 papillary thyroid carcinoma, found the non-surgical approach was competitive on oncological outcomes while delivering substantially lower morbidity. A systematic review and meta-analysis in JAMA Otolaryngology in 2022, examining radiofrequency ablation for papillary microcarcinomas, found very low rates of incomplete ablation and local recurrence, with high rates of primary treatment success.

The management of thyroid nodules has evolved from a paradigm in which the options were either surgery or observation, to one defined by precision imaging, cytology, and molecular diagnostics, with minimally invasive options now available. This sentence from the 2025 AACE Year in Review is accurate, but it understates the speed of the transition. Five years ago, RFA for thyroid cancer outside of clinical trials was unusual in the United States. Today, it is a guideline-acknowledged option. The trajectory is toward it becoming standard of care for carefully selected patients, particularly as the technology improves and more clinicians acquire the technique.

## The Molecular Revolution in Thyroid Cancer

The biological drivers of thyroid malignancy are now characterized at sufficient molecular resolution to stratify risk, guide adjuvant therapy decisions, and — for advanced disease — select targeted treatments.

The major molecular drivers divide roughly by clinical behavior:

**BRAF V600E mutation** is present in approximately 45–60% of papillary thyroid cancers. BRAF V600E activates the MAPK/ERK signaling pathway constitutively, driving unregulated cell proliferation. In differentiated PTC, BRAF mutation correlates with slightly more aggressive behavior, reduced radioiodine uptake, and higher risk of recurrence. In anaplastic thyroid carcinoma, BRAF V600E is the most important actionable target: the combination of dabrafenib (a BRAF inhibitor) and trametinib (a MEK inhibitor) has produced response rates and overall survival improvements in BRAF-mutated ATC that were unimaginable with prior regimens.

**RET/PTC rearrangements** drive a significant minority of papillary thyroid cancers, particularly in post-radiation populations (the Chernobyl cohort, for example, shows dramatically elevated rates of RET/PTC rearrangement-driven PTC). RET inhibitors — selpercatinib and pralsetinib — are now FDA-approved for RET fusion-driven thyroid cancers and medullary thyroid carcinoma, with response rates in the 60–70% range in heavily pre-treated patients.

**RAS mutations** (HRAS, KRAS, NRAS) are the predominant drivers of follicular thyroid cancer and are found in a subset of papillary cancers and poorly differentiated tumors. They tend to correlate with follicular growth pattern and do not respond to BRAF-targeting agents.

**TERT promoter mutations** are found in approximately 7–25% of differentiated thyroid cancers, with increasing frequency in dedifferentiated tumors. Their presence correlates strongly with more aggressive behavior, higher recurrence rates, and worse survival — and they appear to act synergistically with BRAF V600E. The combination of BRAF V600E and TERT promoter mutation identifies a subset of differentiated thyroid cancers with substantially worse prognosis than BRAF or TERT mutation alone.

**ALK fusions, NTRK fusions, and other kinase-activating alterations** occur in smaller subsets and are increasingly targetable with approved agents — larotrectinib and entrectinib for NTRK fusions, lorlatinib and crizotinib for ALK fusions.

The clinical implication of this molecular landscape is a shift away from histological staging as the primary risk-stratification tool toward molecular profiling. A 3 cm papillary thyroid cancer with no BRAF or TERT mutation and no lymph node involvement has a fundamentally different risk profile than a 1.5 cm papillary cancer with both BRAF V600E and a TERT promoter mutation. The 2025 ATA guidelines incorporate this, building molecular data explicitly into risk stratification.

## Molecular Diagnostics: The Indeterminate Nodule Problem

Approximately 20–25% of fine-needle aspiration (FNA) biopsies of thyroid nodules return cytologically indeterminate results — the Bethesda III and IV categories, which correspond to "atypia of undetermined significance" and "follicular neoplasm." Historically, these results led to diagnostic lobectomy in most cases, because cytology alone could not distinguish between benign follicular adenoma and follicular thyroid carcinoma.

Gene expression classifiers — molecular tests performed on biopsy material — have significantly improved the management of indeterminate nodules. The Afirma Gene Sequencing Classifier, Thyroseq v3, and ThyGeNEXT/ThyraMIR are now widely used to triage Bethesda III and IV nodules toward either surgery or observation, depending on the molecular signature.

The Afirma classifier, for example, reports results as "benign" or "suspicious." Afirma-benign results have a negative predictive value for malignancy of approximately 95% in appropriately selected populations — sufficient to defer surgery for the majority of indeterminate nodules. Thyroseq v3 provides information not only on malignancy risk but on specific molecular drivers, which informs surgical planning.

These tests are not perfect. False negatives occur. The test characteristics vary by institutional case mix and baseline malignancy prevalence in the population being tested. But the reduction in diagnostic lobectomies for ultimately benign nodules — with all of the associated morbidity and post-surgical hypothyroidism — represents a meaningful improvement in patient outcomes.

The 2025 Swiss Empa technology that creates three-dimensional AI imaging of thyroid biopsy specimens without destroying the sample adds another dimension: non-destructive assessment that preserves tissue for downstream molecular testing if the initial analysis is insufficient. The integration of AI with molecular diagnostics is still emerging, but the trajectory is toward a diagnostic pipeline that combines imaging, molecular, and cytological information in ways that reduce both false positives (unnecessary surgery) and false negatives (missed cancers).

## Radioactive Iodine: The Recalibration

Radioactive iodine (RAI) ablation — ingesting radioactive I-131, which the thyroid accumulates specifically due to the sodium-iodide symporter, destroying remaining thyroid tissue and micrometastases — has been a cornerstone of differentiated thyroid cancer treatment for decades. The 2025 ATA guidelines signal a significant recalibration of its use.

The core change: RAI is now formally discouraged for low-risk differentiated thyroid cancer after lobectomy or even total thyroidectomy, absent high-risk features. The evidence accumulated over the past decade shows that RAI ablation does not improve disease-specific survival or recurrence rates in low-risk patients, while carrying real morbidity — salivary gland dysfunction, dry eye, radiation-associated secondary malignancies, and reproductive effects in younger patients.

For intermediate and high-risk patients, RAI remains an important and effective tool. But the reflexive post-thyroidectomy RAI that characterized thyroid cancer management through much of the 2000s and 2010s is now formally, guidelines-explicitly, inappropriate for the majority of differentiated thyroid cancer patients.

## The Anaplastic Exception

Anaplastic thyroid carcinoma deserves special attention because the recent therapeutic advances are genuinely dramatic and because the disease itself is so catastrophically severe that any improvement is noteworthy.

ATC is rare — approximately 1–2% of all thyroid cancers — but accounts for approximately 40% of thyroid cancer deaths despite its rarity. The median survival from diagnosis was historically 3–6 months. Five-year survival rates were in the low single digits.

For patients whose ATC harbors BRAF V600E mutations — approximately 40–50% of cases — the combination of dabrafenib and trametinib (marketed as Tafinlar + Mekinist) has produced responses that were previously unheard of: partial or complete responses in 50–60% of BRAF-mutated ATC patients, with median overall survival extending to 18+ months in some series. These are not cures — ATC remains lethal in most cases — but they represent the first meaningful pharmacological improvement in a cancer that had been essentially untreatable. The lesson is what molecular targeting of the right driver can accomplish, and it is already changing the diagnostic workflow for ATC to ensure BRAF testing occurs immediately at diagnosis to identify who can benefit.

---

## What the Next Ten Years Look Like

The thyroid cancer trajectory for the next decade involves several converging technologies: more precise molecular profiling at diagnosis; AI-augmented ultrasound and biopsy pathology; broader access to minimally invasive thermal ablation for selected patients; and, for advanced disease, the continuation of the targeted therapy revolution that has already produced results in BRAF-, RET-, and NTRK-driven cancers.

Universal application of active surveillance for low-risk microcarcinomas, if adopted broadly, would dramatically reduce the net harm currently produced by the overdiagnosis pipeline. Whether the legal environment, the clinical culture, and the patient expectation that "cancer" requires "treatment" will allow this depends on factors outside the laboratory.

The paradox with which this section opened — a disease simultaneously over-treated at its least dangerous end and under-treated at its most dangerous end — is being resolved, slowly, from both directions. The evidence base has done its job. Whether the practice catches up is the perennial question.
