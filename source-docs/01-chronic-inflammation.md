# The Fire That Never Goes Out

*Your immune system is designed to start fires. The problem is, it forgot how to stop.*

**Neuroscience / Longevity · 10 min read**

---

There is a process happening in your body right now that connects heart disease, Alzheimer's, depression, type 2 diabetes, and at least a dozen cancers. It doesn't show up on standard blood panels. Most doctors won't mention it at your annual physical. It has no symptoms you'd recognize as symptoms — no fever, no swelling, no obvious sign that anything is wrong.

It's called chronic low-grade inflammation, and the case that it's the central mechanism of modern disease has never been stronger than it is right now.

This is not inflammation as you know it. When you sprain an ankle, the swelling and heat are acute inflammation — your immune system mobilizing, doing exactly what it evolved to do, then standing down. That process is a triumph of biology. What researchers are describing now is something different: a smoldering, persistent immune activation that never reaches crisis and never fully resolves. A fire that gets set and never quite goes out.

The distinction matters enormously. One is a feature. The other may be quietly killing you.

---

## What Inflammation Actually Is

Before getting to what goes wrong, it helps to understand what's supposed to happen.

When your body detects damage or foreign invaders — a cut, a pathogen, a splinter — specialized immune cells flood the area. They release signaling proteins called cytokines, which recruit more immune cells, increase blood flow, and trigger the characteristic heat, redness, and swelling. The goal is to contain the threat, clear the debris, and begin repair.

This is acute inflammation, and it is one of the most elegant systems in biology. When it works correctly, it resolves in days. The immune system declares victory, anti-inflammatory signals shut down the response, and tissue repair begins.

Chronic inflammation is what happens when that shutdown never comes. The immune system stays activated — not at the level of a full emergency, but enough to sustain a low-level molecular war. Cytokines like interleukin-6 (IL-6), interleukin-1β, and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) circulate at elevated levels. Immune cells patrol tissues that don't need patrolling. The body's internal chemistry tips into a state of perpetual readiness for a threat that isn't there.

The consequences accumulate slowly, over years and decades, in virtually every organ system.

## The Disease Connection

The research linking chronic inflammation to major disease has moved from hypothesis to something approaching consensus with unusual speed.

**Heart disease.** For decades, the dominant story was cholesterol. The new story is more complicated. Inflammation appears to be the mechanism by which arterial plaque becomes dangerous — not the buildup itself, but the immune response to it. A landmark clinical trial called CANTOS found that a drug targeting a single inflammatory pathway (IL-1β) reduced cardiovascular events in high-risk patients by 15%, without touching cholesterol levels. It wasn't a commercial success, but its scientific significance was enormous: it was the first direct evidence that suppressing inflammation, independent of lipids, reduces heart attacks.

**Alzheimer's disease.** The amyloid hypothesis — the idea that Alzheimer's is caused by a buildup of protein plaques — has struggled against a long record of failed clinical trials. An emerging alternative places neuroinflammation at the center of the story. Microglia, the brain's resident immune cells, appear to shift from protective to destructive in chronic inflammatory conditions, contributing to neuronal damage and accelerating cognitive decline. The APOE4 gene variant, the strongest known genetic risk factor for Alzheimer's, is expressed in immune cells, not neurons — a fact that fits far more neatly into an inflammation model than an amyloid one.

**Depression.** The connection between inflammation and depression was once considered fringe. It's not anymore. A significant subset of people with major depression — estimates range from 30 to 50% — show elevated inflammatory markers. People with inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis have dramatically higher rates of depression. Perhaps most compellingly, administering inflammatory cytokines to healthy volunteers reliably produces depressive symptoms: fatigue, social withdrawal, anhedonia, slowed cognition. The inflamed brain, it turns out, behaves like a depressed one.

**Cancer.** The WHO has estimated that chronic infection and inflammation account for approximately 20% of cancers globally. The mechanisms are several: inflammation generates reactive oxygen species that damage DNA, promotes the growth of new blood vessels that feed tumors, and suppresses immune surveillance that would otherwise detect and destroy malignant cells. H. pylori causes stomach cancer. Hepatitis C causes liver cancer. Chronic bowel inflammation significantly raises colorectal cancer risk. The common thread is not the specific pathogen but the inflammatory environment it creates.

**Type 2 diabetes.** Adipose tissue — body fat — is not metabolically inert. It secretes inflammatory cytokines, particularly in excess amounts. Obesity drives systemic inflammation, which impairs insulin signaling, which worsens glucose regulation, which adds more metabolic stress. The relationship is bidirectional and self-reinforcing. Inflammation isn't just a consequence of metabolic disease; it appears to be a driver.

## What's Causing It

The most unsettling aspect of chronic inflammation is that its primary causes are features of modern life, not rare exposures or genetic misfortune.

**Diet.** Ultra-processed foods — which now account for more than half of the caloric intake in countries like the United States and United Kingdom — are among the most reliably pro-inflammatory inputs a human body can receive. Refined carbohydrates spike blood glucose, triggering inflammatory signaling. Industrial seed oils high in omega-6 fatty acids shift the body's lipid balance toward pro-inflammatory eicosanoids. Emulsifiers and artificial additives appear to compromise the gut lining, allowing bacterial components to leak into systemic circulation and trigger immune responses. The Mediterranean diet's association with lower inflammatory markers is, in this light, not a mystery: it is the rough inverse of the above.

**Sleep deprivation.** Even a single night of poor sleep measurably elevates inflammatory markers. Chronic sleep restriction — less than six hours per night, sustained over weeks — produces levels of IL-6 and TNF-α comparable to low-grade inflammatory disease. The mechanism involves the immune system's circadian regulation: inflammatory processes are normally suppressed during deep sleep as part of the body's repair cycle. Truncate the sleep, truncate the repair.

**Chronic stress.** The stress hormone cortisol is, acutely, anti-inflammatory. The body deploys it to modulate immune responses during threats. But chronic stress desensitizes immune cells to cortisol's signal — a process called glucocorticoid resistance — leaving the inflammatory response without its normal brake. Remarkably, this desensitization can be detected at the cellular level in people reporting high chronic stress, even when their cortisol levels look normal on a blood test.

**Sedentary behavior.** Exercise is one of the most potent anti-inflammatory interventions known. Muscle contractions release a class of signaling proteins called myokines, several of which directly suppress inflammatory cytokines. Skeletal muscle, when regularly used, functions as an endocrine organ that actively regulates systemic inflammation. A body that doesn't move loses that regulation.

**Environmental exposures.** Microplastics, air pollution, and persistent organic pollutants have each been shown to activate inflammatory pathways. PFAS compounds — the so-called forever chemicals found in non-stick cookware, food packaging, and water supplies — trigger immune dysregulation. These aren't theoretical risks; the exposures are near-universal in industrialized populations.

## The Measurement Problem

One reason chronic inflammation has taken so long to be taken seriously as a clinical priority is that it's genuinely difficult to measure in ways that translate to practice.

The most widely used marker is high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP), a protein produced by the liver in response to inflammatory cytokines. It's inexpensive, widely available, and has strong predictive value for cardiovascular events and all-cause mortality. A level below 1 mg/L is generally considered low risk; 1–3 mg/L is moderate; above 3 mg/L is high. Most standard physicals don't include it.

Other markers with emerging evidence include:
- **IL-6** — one of the primary upstream cytokines, more sensitive than CRP but less standardized
- **Fibrinogen** — a clotting protein that rises with inflammation and predicts cardiovascular risk
- **White blood cell count** — crude but informative; chronically elevated counts in the normal range correlate with worse outcomes
- **Omega-6/omega-3 ratio** — a dietary marker of the lipid environment that governs prostaglandin production

The honest caveat is that no single marker fully captures the complexity of systemic inflammatory status. A panel approach is more informative than any individual test, and interpretation requires clinical context. But some measurement is substantially better than none, and the current clinical default — measuring nothing unless something has already gone wrong — is increasingly difficult to justify.

## What Actually Works

The research on anti-inflammatory interventions has both genuine good news and a lot of noise to cut through.

**What the evidence supports:**

*Omega-3 fatty acids.* The most consistent supplementation evidence in this space. Marine-sourced EPA and DHA measurably shift the lipid balance toward anti-inflammatory pathways. Effect sizes are modest at lower doses; studies using 2–4g daily of combined EPA/DHA show more robust effects on IL-6 and TNF-α.

*Exercise.* Non-negotiable, and there's no supplement substitute. Both aerobic exercise and resistance training reduce inflammatory markers, with Zone 2 aerobic work (sustained moderate intensity) showing particularly strong effects on systemic inflammation over time. The dose-response relationship appears to be real: more is better, up to a point that most people will never reach.

*Sleep.* Treating sleep as a recoverable luxury is exactly wrong. Seven to nine hours per night for most adults isn't a lifestyle preference; it's a biological requirement for inflammatory homeostasis. The research on sleep extension — deliberately adding sleep time — shows measurable reductions in inflammatory markers within weeks.

*Dietary pattern.* The evidence for specific anti-inflammatory foods is messier than it's often presented. But the evidence for overall dietary pattern is robust. Less ultra-processed food, more vegetables, legumes, fish, and olive oil, fewer refined carbohydrates and industrial seed oils — the Mediterranean pattern, broadly interpreted — consistently associates with lower inflammatory markers and better health outcomes.

*Stress regulation.* The mechanism is real; the interventions are less standardized. Mindfulness-based stress reduction has the strongest evidence base among behavioral interventions for reducing inflammatory markers. The effect sizes are not large, but they are replicable.

**What's not well-established:**

Most individual supplements marketed as "anti-inflammatory" — curcumin, quercetin, resveratrol — have impressive results in cell culture and disappointing results in humans. Bioavailability is typically the problem: these compounds don't survive the digestive process in forms that produce systemic effects at realistic doses. Some formulations have improved this picture, but the evidence remains thin relative to the marketing.

## Why This Matters More Than Most Health Topics

There is something philosophically uncomfortable about chronic inflammation as a concept. It doesn't have a clean story arc — no infection to fight, no obvious enemy, no single drug to take. It's the product of how we eat, move, sleep, and exist in a polluted, stressed, sedentary world. The interventions aren't dramatic.

But that's also what makes it unusual among health topics: the evidence for prevention is actually quite strong. The mechanisms are known. The behavioral modifications are not exotic. For most people, the inflammatory trajectory is not fixed.

The fire can be banked, if not extinguished. That's a more actionable message than most medicine offers.
