# Your Body Has a Gear It's Forgotten How to Use

*Metabolic flexibility — the ability to switch cleanly between burning fat and burning glucose — is one of the most important markers of metabolic health. Most people living on modern diets have lost it. Here's what that actually means, and what it takes to get it back.*

**Nutrition · 9 min read**

---

Here is a thought experiment that gets at something important.

Imagine a car that can run on either petrol or electric power — a hybrid. In normal operation, the engine switches between them seamlessly depending on conditions: electric at low speeds, petrol at high demand, a blend in between. The transitions are smooth, efficient, and largely invisible to the driver.

Now imagine the same car, but the switch mechanism has seized. It's stuck on petrol. It can still drive, but it burns through fuel inefficiently, generates excess heat, and struggles at the operating conditions where the electric motor should be doing most of the work. Nothing has failed catastrophically. The car still moves. But it's running wrong.

That is, approximately, what happens to human metabolism under the conditions most people now live in. And the name for the stuck switch is metabolic inflexibility.

---

## What Metabolic Flexibility Actually Is

The body runs on two primary fuels: glucose (from carbohydrates) and fatty acids (from fat). Under healthy conditions, the mix it uses shifts continuously based on what's available, what the current activity demands, and how recently you've eaten.

After a carbohydrate-rich meal, insulin rises, glucose floods the bloodstream, and the body appropriately prioritizes burning glucose. During fasting, sleep, or low-intensity exercise, insulin falls, glucose availability drops, and the body switches to fat oxidation to meet its energy needs. During sustained aerobic exercise, the mix shifts again depending on intensity.

This is metabolic flexibility: the capacity to make those transitions rapidly, completely, and in proportion to what the situation calls for.

Metabolic inflexibility is what happens when the transitions fail. The most common pattern in insulin-resistant individuals is an impaired ability to suppress fat oxidation when glucose is abundant, and an impaired ability to upregulate fat oxidation when glucose is scarce. The body is stuck in a metabolic no-man's-land — not efficiently burning either fuel, relying instead on an increasing stream of dietary carbohydrate because the machinery for fat oxidation has rusted from disuse.

The consequences accumulate at the mitochondrial level. When fuel is delivered to mitochondria in excess and in a constant unvarying stream — as it is in people who eat frequently, eat high-carbohydrate diets, and never give the fat oxidation pathways a chance to activate — reducing equivalents pile up in the mitochondria, the membrane potential rises, and reactive oxygen species are generated at elevated rates. Cell injury follows. Insulin resistance deepens. Inflammation increases.

## How the Modern Diet Broke the Switch

Metabolic inflexibility is not primarily a genetic condition. It is the predictable result of eating patterns that keep insulin elevated almost continuously.

The combination of frequent eating and high-carbohydrate, high-glycaemic food has produced, in most industrialised populations, a metabolic environment in which the fat-burning machinery rarely gets activated. Skeletal muscle fat oxidation capacity — the most metabolically important tissue for this purpose — requires regular exercise and regular periods of low insulin to stay calibrated. When neither happens, fat oxidation capacity declines through a process of enzymatic downregulation. The muscle loses the mitochondrial density and the relevant enzyme expression to efficiently oxidise fat, even when fat is the fuel it should theoretically be using.

This is the physiological basis of a pattern many people will recognise: persistent hunger even after meals, difficulty going more than a few hours without eating, energy crashes in the mid-afternoon, poor performance during any physical activity that lasts more than about 30 minutes. These are the experiential signatures of a metabolism that has lost the ability to access its largest fuel store — the 40,000-plus calories of fat that even a lean person carries.

The fat is there. The machinery to use it has gone quiet.

## Why Inflexibility Matters Beyond Diabetes

Metabolic inflexibility is most commonly discussed in the context of insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes, where the impairment is severe enough to produce clinical symptoms. This framing undersells the problem.

Insulin resistance exists on a continuum, and the metabolic dysfunction associated with inflexibility occurs well before the threshold for a clinical diagnosis. Studies have found that reduced fat oxidation capacity is detectable in people with normal fasting glucose and normal HbA1c — standard markers that the standard physical uses to define "no diabetes." These individuals are metabolically compromised in ways that the routine tests miss.

The downstream consequences extend across every organ system that depends on efficient energy production. Cardiac function. Liver health. Brain metabolism. Muscle performance. The mitochondrial inefficiency and chronic low-grade inflammation that accompany inflexibility contribute to the same disease processes documented throughout this series — cardiovascular disease, neurodegeneration, depression.

Metabolic flexibility is also a predictor of how well the body handles metabolic stress — a large meal, a period of illness, a caloric surplus, an intense exercise session. A flexible metabolism absorbs these challenges and returns to equilibrium quickly. An inflexible one is destabilised by them, generating larger glucose spikes, longer periods of elevated inflammation, and slower recovery.

The technical measure of metabolic flexibility — the respiratory exchange ratio (RER), which reflects the mix of fuels being oxidised at any moment — has been correlated with cardiovascular outcomes, all-cause mortality, and measures of healthy ageing. It is not a research curiousity. It is a meaningful marker of how well the body's fundamental energy system is functioning.

## The Keto Overclaim

At this point, a reader familiar with wellness media will be expecting the segue to ketogenic diets. After all, the language of metabolic flexibility has been aggressively colonised by low-carbohydrate evangelists who frame keto as the path to becoming "fat-adapted" — a state in which the body has fully switched its preference to fat burning.

The evidence supports a more nuanced position.

Ketogenic and low-carbohydrate diets do increase fat oxidation capacity, consistently and measurably. People on chronic keto diets become more efficient at burning fat. This is real. It is also not quite the same thing as metabolic flexibility, which is specifically the ability to switch fluidly between fuels.

A person who has been on a strict ketogenic diet for months has excellent fat oxidation but often markedly impaired glucose oxidation — their muscles have downregulated the glycolytic enzyme activity that efficient carbohydrate metabolism requires. They have traded one form of inflexibility for another. Introduce a high-carbohydrate challenge and their glucose disposal is sluggish, their insulin response exaggerated, and their metabolic performance during high-intensity exercise — which requires rapid glycolytic flux — deteriorates noticeably.

True metabolic flexibility is the ability to use both fuels efficiently. The keto version of "metabolic flexibility" is, in practice, metabolic preference — a preference so strongly biased toward fat that glucose oxidation is compromised. That is not what the research on healthy aging and disease prevention is pointing to.

## What Actually Restores It

The interventions with robust evidence for improving genuine metabolic flexibility are not revolutionary. They are, however, specific enough to be worth describing precisely.

**Exercise, particularly aerobic exercise at moderate-to-high intensity.** This is the most potent intervention known for restoring skeletal muscle fat oxidation capacity. Exercise increases mitochondrial density through PGC-1α signalling, upregulates the relevant fat oxidation enzymes, and improves insulin sensitivity in the tissues that matter most. The improvements are measurable within weeks. They require ongoing stimulus to be maintained. This is the metabolic adaptation that Zone 2 training is most correctly credited with supporting, with the caveat — addressed in the adjacent article in this series — that higher intensities may be more efficient per unit of time for achieving this adaptation.

**Time-restricted eating.** The research on intermittent fasting and metabolic flexibility is methodologically messy, but the mechanism is clear: periods without food allow insulin to fall, glycogen to deplete, and fat oxidation pathways to activate. Whether a strict fasting protocol is required for this benefit, or whether simply reducing eating frequency and extending overnight fasts achieves a comparable result, is not definitively established. What is clear is that eating continuously throughout the day, or eating frequently enough to prevent insulin from ever falling to baseline, actively impairs the activation of fat-burning pathways.

**Reducing refined carbohydrates and ultra-processed foods.** Not eliminating carbohydrates — there is no good evidence that dietary carbohydrates per se impair metabolic flexibility in the context of otherwise healthy eating patterns. The issue is the glycaemic profile: foods that produce large, rapid insulin spikes maintain the conditions under which fat oxidation is suppressed. High-fibre carbohydrates, consumed as part of meals rather than as continuous snacking, produce a very different insulin environment than processed carbohydrates consumed frequently throughout the day.

**Sleep.** During sleep, the body spends extended hours in a fasted, low-insulin state — precisely the conditions that activate fat oxidation and allow the machinery to stay calibrated. Chronic sleep deprivation impairs insulin sensitivity, disrupts the metabolic flexibility that overnight fasting supports, and increases reliance on glucose as the default fuel even in conditions where fat should be primary.

## The Measurement Problem

One reason metabolic flexibility hasn't achieved the clinical prominence it deserves is that direct measurement is expensive and inconvenient. The gold standard — indirect calorimetry during an insulin clamp procedure — requires hospital-grade equipment, specialized personnel, and an elaborate protocol. This is not a routine clinical test.

Consumer devices have tried to fill the gap, with limited success. Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs), now widely marketed to non-diabetic "biohackers," measure one variable — glucose — that reflects one aspect of metabolic response. A CGM tells you how quickly your glucose rises and falls after eating, which is informative. It does not tell you about fat oxidation, which is the other half of the flexibility equation.

Resting metabolic rate measurements combined with a dietary challenge, performed in some specialist clinics, offer a more complete picture. For most people, the practical proxy for metabolic flexibility is the collection of indirect signals: how much energy variability do you experience between meals; how well do you perform physically during fasted exercise; how severe are your cravings and mood fluctuations around food; how quickly do you recover metabolic stability after a high-carbohydrate meal?

These are imprecise, but they are informative. And for most people, simply addressing the behavioral inputs — exercise, eating patterns, sleep, food quality — is more productive than pursuing laboratory measurement of a system that will improve predictably if those inputs improve.

## The Underneath of Everything

Metabolic flexibility doesn't have the cultural cachet of a named diet or a biohacking protocol. It isn't photogenic. There is no product to sell that reliably restores it. The wellness industry has therefore found it less useful as a marketing vehicle than intermittent fasting apps, CGM subscriptions, or ketone meters — all of which address specific aspects of the metabolic picture while leaving others unaddressed.

But in terms of what the research points to as foundational to long-term metabolic health, flexibility sits close to the center. A body that can shift cleanly between fuels — that doesn't require continuous carbohydrate to function, that can access its fat stores during the hours between meals, that responds to insulin promptly and recovers quickly from metabolic challenges — is a body with substantially lower risk across a wide range of conditions.

The switch is not broken. It is, in most cases, simply stuck. And the things that unstick it are the same unsexy interventions that appear in every evidence-based health article that is being honest about what actually works.
