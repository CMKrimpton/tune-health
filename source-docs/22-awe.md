# The Neuroscience of Awe

*Something happens when you stand at the rim of the Grand Canyon, hear a piece of music that opens a door in your chest, or watch a human being do something you genuinely didn't think was possible. Scientists have spent twenty years trying to understand what that something is — and the findings are, appropriately enough, a little overwhelming.*

**Neuroscience · Mental Health · 8 min read**

---

There is a specific feeling that doesn't quite fit the standard emotional categories. It isn't happiness, exactly, or beauty, or joy, though it borrows from all three. It involves a sense of vastness — something perceived as larger than your current frame of reference — and a momentary dissolution of self-concern. Your ordinary preoccupations, the mental tape loop of self-assessment and social navigation that runs most of the time, goes quiet. Something larger fills its place.

This is awe. And for most of human history, its exploration was left to philosophers, theologians, poets, and composers. The scientific study of it barely existed before the early 2000s. What has been built since constitutes one of the more surprising bodies of research in positive psychology: an emotion that reduces inflammation, quiets the brain's self-referential networks, expands time perception, increases generosity, and reduces depressive symptoms — and that most adults in most industrialized countries experience only rarely and often accidentally.

---

## Defining the Indefinable (Approximately)

Awe, in the scientific definition developed by Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt, arises in response to stimuli that are perceived as vast — physically, conceptually, or socially — and that require an update to the perceiver's current mental frameworks. The vastness can be spatial (a mountain range, a night sky), temporal (deep time, evolutionary history), conceptual (a mathematical proof, a paradigm-shifting idea), or social (extraordinary skill, moral courage, collective synchrony).

The "need for accommodation" component distinguishes awe from mere beauty or pleasantness. Awe occurs when something exceeds the frame you brought to it — when the experience requires you to revise your sense of scale, expand your understanding, or update your model of what is possible.

This definition predicts the diversity of awe triggers: nature, music, great art, witnessing exceptional human performance, encountering moral heroism, participating in collective rituals, scientific revelation, religious experience. These seem very different, but they share the structural features of scale-exceeding vastness and the need for cognitive accommodation.

Research across more than 25 cultures has found consistent patterns of awe experience across populations — common triggers, common phenomenological signatures, common physiological correlates — which suggests awe is a fundamental feature of human emotional architecture rather than a culturally specific response.

## The Neuroscience

The most significant neural signature of awe is reduced activity in the default mode network — the brain's self-referential system that generates the continuous inner narrative of the self, its concerns, its comparisons, and its relationship to the social world.

This is the mechanism of what awe experiencers describe as ego dissolution — the self becoming smaller, or temporarily irrelevant, in the face of something larger. The DMN quieting in awe is not the same as falling asleep or losing consciousness; other networks remain active or become more so. It's more specific: the brain's apparatus for self-monitoring and self-centered processing temporarily cedes priority to outward experience.

The physiological profile is equally specific. Awe activates the parasympathetic nervous system — the rest-and-digest mode — increasing vagal tone and reducing sympathetic arousal. Heart rate typically drops. Respiration deepens. Goosebumps and chills (piloerection) occur more frequently with awe than with other positive emotions, suggesting a distinct physiological signature. Oxytocin release is associated with awe experiences that involve witnessing human connection or social beauty.

Most remarkably, awe is associated with reduced levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines — particularly interleukin-6 (IL-6), one of the primary inflammatory signaling molecules implicated in chronic disease. In a study by Stellar and colleagues, among seven positive emotions measured, awe was the strongest predictor of lower IL-6 levels. A particularly awe-filled day, even a single day, was associated with lower inflammatory markers independent of the person's baseline tendency to experience awe.

## The Anti-Inflammatory Emotion

The connection between awe and inflammation deserves particular attention, given the prominence of chronic inflammation in the broader picture of modern health.

The vagal activation associated with awe partially explains the anti-inflammatory effect. As discussed in the vagus nerve article in this series, the vagus nerve's inflammatory reflex — its capacity to suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine production through acetylcholine signaling — is activated when vagal tone is high. Awe, by consistently increasing vagal tone, may contribute to lower systemic inflammation through this pathway.

The reduced self-referential processing also likely plays a role. The default mode network, when hyperactive — as it is in chronic stress, rumination, and depression — drives sustained HPA axis activation and inflammatory signaling. A state that quiets the DMN, even temporarily, interrupts that cascade.

This mechanism helps explain a 2025 randomized controlled trial published in *Scientific Reports*: an awe intervention delivered to long COVID patients in online group sessions produced significant reductions in depression and stress symptoms, and increases in wellbeing, compared to a control group over the same period. Long COVID, as discussed elsewhere in this series, involves sustained neuroinflammation — the same target that awe, through vagal and DMN mechanisms, appears to modulate.

The clinical application is in early stages. But the mechanistic plausibility is now substantial.

## Awe Expands Time

One of the more counterintuitive findings in awe research is its effect on time perception. Studies have consistently found that awe experiences make time feel more expansive — people report feeling less rushed, more patient, and more willing to spend time on activities without immediate reward.

The proposed mechanism: awe demands cognitive accommodation, and accommodation requires processing time. The brain allocates more attentional resources to the present moment when it is processing something that exceeds its current framework. This expanded present-moment attention creates a subjective experience of more time — the reverse of the time-compression of familiar, routinized experience.

The downstream effects are significant. People in awe states show more deliberate decision-making, greater willingness to volunteer time for others, reduced impatience, and higher life satisfaction scores in momentary assessments. In a time-pressured culture where the chronic experience of "not enough time" drives a significant proportion of anxiety and poor decision-making, a state that restores the perception of temporal abundance has practical value beyond its hedonic pleasantness.

## Prosocial Cascade

The research on awe and social behavior documents a consistent pattern: people in awe states, or who have recently experienced awe, behave more generously, show greater intellectual humility, make less self-centered judgments, feel stronger connection to collective social identity, and show greater curiosity about the world.

These effects are partly explained by the DMN quieting — when the brain's self-monitoring system is less active, self-interested decision-making correspondingly declines. They are also partly explained by the sense of smallness that awe characteristically produces. Feeling small in the face of something vast doesn't typically feel threatening; it feels, in the classic awe experience, like relief. A brief release from the burden of maintaining the self's constant self-assessment.

Awe triggered by other people's moral courage or exceptional skill — what Keltner calls "social awe" — shows particular effects on moral motivation. Witnessing moral heroism reliably produces a state that has been described as "moral elevation" — a warm, open, motivated sense that extraordinary goodness is possible, accompanied by increased prosocial intentions and actions.

## Where Awe Is Found, and Where It Isn't

Keltner's cross-cultural diary research found that people reliably experience awe through:

- Nature — vastness, wildness, meteorological phenomena, animal behavior
- Other people's exceptional skill, knowledge, or virtue
- Music — particularly at moments of unexpected emotional intensity
- Collective ritual — synchronized movement, ceremony, shared experience
- Epiphany — when an idea or understanding suddenly reorganizes a mental framework
- Great art, architecture, or crafted beauty
- Life transitions — birth, death, transformation

What the research also found is that in modern industrialized environments, awe experiences have become less frequent. The designed environment of most urban adult life — built for efficiency, comfort, and familiarity — is systematically poor at generating the vastness cues that trigger awe. Nature is less accessible. Ritual is less present. The pace of contemporary life makes cognitive accommodation feel like a cost rather than an invitation.

The average American adult, in Keltner's research, reported experiencing awe fewer than three times per week; many reported far less. Given that even a single awe experience reduces inflammatory markers for that day, the secular trend away from awe may have health consequences that extend beyond the obvious quality-of-life dimension.

The prescription, such as it is, is not exotic: regular contact with natural environments, with music listened to rather than used as background, with collective experiences that generate synchrony, with large ideas engaged in their full complexity. These are not extraordinary demands. They are conditions that industrialized modern life has slowly, without intention, been eliminating from daily experience.

Getting them back is not a spiritual practice. It is, apparently, a health recommendation.
