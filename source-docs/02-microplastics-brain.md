# You Are Mostly Plastic Now

*Microplastics have been found in human brains, hearts, placentas, and blood. The science of what they do once they get there is just beginning — and it isn't reassuring.*

**Environmental Health · 9 min read**

---

In 2024, a team of researchers at the University of New Mexico published a paper that quietly rewrote the terms of the microplastics conversation. They weren't studying fish. They weren't studying water or soil or Arctic ice cores. They were studying human brains.

What they found was that brain tissue from autopsy samples contained, on average, about 4,800 micrograms of plastic per gram of tissue — the equivalent, by weight, of a plastic spoon distributed across the brain. Samples from 2024 contained roughly 50% more plastic than samples from 2016. And brains from people who had been diagnosed with dementia before death contained significantly higher concentrations than those who hadn't.

That last finding has not been established as causal. The researchers were careful to say so. But the correlation was robust enough, and the mechanism plausible enough, that "preliminary" is doing some heavy lifting in describing where this science now stands.

Microplastics are no longer an environmental problem that happens to affect humans incidentally. They are a human health problem with an environmental origin — and the exposure is, at this point, essentially universal.

---

## How They Get In

Microplastics are defined as plastic particles smaller than 5 millimeters. Nanoplastics are a subset: fragments smaller than a micrometer, some measured in nanometers. The distinction matters because size determines biological behavior. Larger microplastics tend to accumulate in the gut and are largely excreted. Nanoplastics are small enough to cross cell membranes, translocate into the bloodstream, and deposit in organs.

The sources are extensive to the point of being difficult to enumerate. They include:

- **Food and water.** Plastic packaging continuously sheds particles into its contents, particularly under heat. A single plastic water bottle can release millions of nanoplastic particles per liter. Seafood accumulates microplastics throughout the marine food chain. Tap water contains them in most cities that have been tested.
- **Air.** Synthetic fabrics shed plastic microfibers with every wash cycle; those fibers enter household dust and are inhaled. Outdoor air, particularly in urban environments, carries plastic particles from tire wear, industrial emissions, and degrading waste.
- **Food preparation.** Cutting on plastic cutting boards generates fragments. Heating food in plastic containers dramatically accelerates leaching. Scratched non-stick pans shed fluoropolymers with every use. (An alumi article on this exists: "Pan-demic" covers the PFAS angle; the microplastic angle is distinct.)
- **Salt and soil.** Sea salt is one of the most contaminated food sources. Agricultural soils treated with sewage sludge contain high concentrations of microplastics that enter crops.

The estimated human ingestion rate is staggering. Studies vary, but the median estimates suggest adults consume somewhere between 74,000 and 121,000 microplastic particles per year through diet alone — more if they drink bottled water. Inhalation adds substantially to that figure. A 2019 study estimated that people who drink predominantly bottled water consume approximately 90,000 additional particles annually compared to those who drink tap water.

We are not talking about trace exposures.

## Where They End Up

The original assumption was that microplastics were largely inert — that they passed through the digestive system without meaningful absorption and were excreted without consequence. That assumption has not survived contact with the research.

Microplastics have now been detected in:
- Human blood (2022, a peer-reviewed first)
- The placenta — including fetal-side tissue
- Breast milk
- Lung tissue
- Testicular tissue
- Cardiac tissue, including inside arterial plaques
- Liver, kidney, and spleen
- And, as noted, the brain

The presence of microplastics in arterial plaques generated significant clinical attention in 2024, when a study published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* found that patients whose plaques contained detectable levels of polyethylene or polyvinyl chloride had a significantly higher risk of heart attack, stroke, and death over the subsequent three years compared to patients whose plaques were plastic-free. The study was observational and the mechanism unproven, but it was the most direct clinical evidence to date that plastic accumulation is not biologically neutral.

## What They Do

The mechanisms by which microplastics cause harm are increasingly understood, and they operate through several pathways simultaneously.

**Direct physical damage.** Plastic particles, particularly angular microplastics, cause mechanical irritation to tissues. In the gut, this disrupts the mucosal lining. In lung tissue, it triggers local inflammatory responses similar to those seen with other inhaled particulates.

**Chemical leaching.** Plastics are not pure polymers. They contain a range of additives — plasticizers, flame retardants, stabilizers, colorants — many of which are biologically active. Phthalates and bisphenols, both widely used in plastics, are endocrine disruptors: they interfere with hormone signaling at extremely low concentrations. BPA's replacement chemicals (BPS, BPF) appear to have similar or worse hormonal effects, despite having been marketed as safer alternatives.

**Inflammatory activation.** This is where the brain story becomes particularly significant. Microplastics — especially at nanoscale — trigger innate immune responses wherever they deposit. In brain tissue, this activates microglia, the resident immune cells that, under chronic activation, become neurotoxic. The pattern of inflammation associated with microplastic exposure in preclinical models resembles early Alzheimer's and Parkinson's pathology in ways that are uncomfortable to dismiss.

**Microbiome disruption.** The gut microbiome is exquisitely sensitive to its chemical environment. Microplastics alter its composition, reducing the diversity and abundance of beneficial bacteria while enriching pro-inflammatory species. Given the centrality of the gut-brain axis to mood, cognition, and systemic inflammation, this disruption has downstream effects that extend well beyond digestion.

## The Brain Question

The New Mexico brain tissue study was not the only research pointing toward neurological consequences. A 2026 review in *Neuroscience News* summarized several lines of converging evidence:

In animal models, nanoplastic exposure produces measurable cognitive deficits — reduced performance on memory and learning tasks — along with elevated markers of neuroinflammation and oxidative stress. The dose levels used in these studies are not dramatically above what humans encounter through routine exposure, which is part of what makes the literature uncomfortable to read.

In human epidemiological data, associations between plastic chemical exposure (typically measured via urinary phthalate or bisphenol metabolites) and cognitive decline, ADHD symptoms in children, and neurodevelopmental delays have been reported across multiple independent cohorts.

The blood-brain barrier, the structure that regulates what enters brain tissue from the bloodstream, is not impervious to nanoplastics. Studies have demonstrated that nanoparticles below 100 nanometers can cross it — and that the barrier's integrity is further compromised by the inflammatory cascade that plastic particles trigger in the cerebrovascular endothelium. It's a compounding problem: the particles weaken the barrier that was supposed to keep them out.

The dementia correlation in the New Mexico brain data has not been replicated at scale. It may not hold up. But the mechanistic plausibility — neuroinflammation, oxidative stress, endocrine disruption, microbiome disruption all converging on brain tissue — is the kind of convergence that tends to predict findings that do hold up.

## What Can Actually Be Done

This is the section where the honest answer is uncomfortable: individual mitigation is partial at best. You cannot opt out of microplastic exposure the way you can choose not to smoke. The contamination of air, water, soil, and most food systems is systemic. At current emission rates, it will get worse before any meaningful remediation occurs.

That said, the difference between median exposure and low-exposure behavior is not trivial.

**Meaningful reductions:**
- Replace plastic water bottles and food storage with glass or stainless steel. The contribution of food and beverage packaging to total load is among the highest and most controllable.
- Avoid heating food in plastic. Migration rates increase dramatically with temperature — even containers labeled "microwave safe" shed significantly more particles when heated.
- Filter tap water through a quality reverse osmosis or ceramic filter. Studies have found these reduce microplastic content in water by 80–95%.
- Ventilate and vacuum frequently. Household dust is a significant inhalation source, and regular cleaning measurably reduces airborne particle load.
- Reduce ultra-processed food consumption — not only for the metabolic reasons covered elsewhere, but because heavily processed foods involve more plastic contact at higher temperatures throughout manufacturing and packaging.

**Limited value:**
- Avoiding sea salt (already switched to mostly microplastics) — worthwhile but a minor contributor to total load.
- "BPA-free" labels — meaningful only if replacements are actually safer, which current evidence does not confirm.
- Air purifiers — help with larger airborne particles but are largely ineffective against nanoplastics.

**The systemic lever:**
Individual behavior change matters at the margins. The real variable is regulatory and industrial. Extended producer responsibility legislation, microplastic filtration requirements for wastewater treatment, and restrictions on single-use plastics in food contact applications are the interventions that would actually move the needle at population level. Several European jurisdictions are further along this path than others.

## The Honest Assessment

There is a tendency in health journalism to oscillate between two positions on emerging environmental health risks: confident alarm or confident dismissal. The microplastics story doesn't support either posture right now.

What the evidence says is this: the exposures are real, universal, and increasing. Microplastics accumulate in human organs including the brain. Several mechanisms by which they cause harm are biologically plausible and supported by preclinical data. Human epidemiological associations with disease are present but not yet definitive. The time scales of these exposures are long enough that we may be studying the early stages of a health burden whose full shape won't be clear for decades.

None of that is reason for panic. It is, however, reason to take the question seriously — and reason to be skeptical of anyone who tells you, with confidence, that there is nothing to worry about.

The plastics industry said the same thing about PFAS for thirty years.
