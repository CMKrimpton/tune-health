# Why Everyone Is Going Nearsighted

*Myopia is exploding globally, affecting people at younger ages and higher rates than any previous generation. For decades, screens got the blame. New research says the real culprit is something subtler — and the implications are stranger than they first appear.*

**Neuroscience · Environmental Health · 7 min read**

---

Something is happening to human vision at a pace and scale that has no precedent in recorded medical history.

Fifty years ago, roughly 25% of the world's population was myopic — unable to see clearly at a distance, corrected by glasses or contact lenses. Today, the figure is approximately 34%, and rising. In East Asia, where the trend is most extreme, rates in young adults now exceed 80–90% in urban populations. In Singapore, the myopia rate in military conscripts — a proxy for young adults — went from around 25% in the 1970s to over 80% by 2000.

The speed of this change rules out genetics as the primary explanation. Genetic profiles don't shift that quickly. Something environmental is driving one of the most visible transformations in human sensory health in the modern era.

The obvious answer, the one that parents and ophthalmologists and tech critics have repeated for years, is screens. We're staring at them constantly. Our eyes are exhausted by near work. The devices are responsible.

The data, it turns out, doesn't really support this. And what the data does support is, in some respects, considerably more unsettling — because it points to a change in the fundamental conditions under which modern humans exist, not merely a device that could be restricted.

---

## The Near Work Hypothesis, and Why It Falls Short

The theory that myopia is caused by excessive near work — reading, writing, screens — has intuitive appeal and a long history. It has also been tested directly, and the results are not as supportive as its persistence in public discourse would suggest.

If near work were the primary driver, myopia rates should be closely correlated with reading rates and screen time across populations. They are not — at least not cleanly. Rural East Asian populations with high literacy rates have lower myopia rates than urban populations in the same countries. Within urban populations, children who read heavily but spend time outdoors show lower myopia rates than children who read less but spend more time indoors. Screen time and myopia correlate, but the relationship weakens substantially when outdoor time is controlled for.

The controlled trials have been more direct. Studies that randomly assigned children to additional outdoor time — without changing their near work, their reading, or their screen use — found significant reductions in myopia development and progression.

The operative variable is not near work. It is outdoor exposure.

---

## The Light Hypothesis

The research now points toward ambient light intensity as the primary environmental driver of myopia — and specifically to the gap between outdoor light and indoor light.

Outdoor light on a clear day measures approximately 100,000 lux. An overcast day might produce 10,000 lux. Indoor environments, even brightly lit ones, typically produce 100–500 lux. The difference is not marginal; it is two to three orders of magnitude. The human eye evolved under outdoor light conditions. The indoor environment is, photometrically, a different world.

The proposed mechanism involves a neurotransmitter called dopamine, which is produced in the retina in response to bright light. Retinal dopamine plays a key role in regulating axial eye growth — the process by which the eyeball elongates during development. Normal axial growth produces a focal point precisely at the retina, resulting in clear vision at all distances. Excessive elongation pushes the focal point in front of the retina, producing myopia.

When ambient light levels are high, retinal dopamine production is high, and axial growth is appropriately regulated. When light levels are chronically low — as they are in most indoor environments — dopamine production is suppressed, the regulatory signal weakens, and the eye elongates beyond the optimal length.

This model explains several otherwise puzzling observations. It explains why outdoor time is protective regardless of the activity — whether children are playing sport, walking, or simply sitting outside. It explains why the protective effect of outdoor time is stronger during critical developmental windows (ages 6–14) when eye growth is most active. It explains why myopia is an urban and indoor-economy phenomenon — city children spend less time outdoors; students in academic pressure systems in East Asia spend extraordinary proportions of their days under artificial light.

---

## The Screens Are a Proxy

In this framework, screen time's apparent correlation with myopia is largely a proxy for the behavior that screens replace: outdoor activity. Children spending hours on devices are hours not spent outdoors. The device is not the problem; the indoors is.

This distinction matters enormously for intervention. "Use screens less" and "spend more time outdoors" are very different recommendations with very different feasibility profiles and very different mechanisms. The research on outdoor time as intervention has produced effect sizes large enough to be clinically meaningful — a 2018 Chinese school-based study found that adding 40 minutes of outdoor time per school day reduced the rate of new myopia onset by approximately 23% over three years. No comparable screen restriction trial has produced effects of that magnitude.

The practical implication is that the conversation around children's screens — which is enormous, politically charged, and often poorly evidenced in other domains — may have assigned screens a causal role in myopia that belongs to the underlying behavior of indoor confinement.

---

## The Evolutionary Dimension

There is a deeper layer to this story that the pure optometric framing misses.

The human visual system did not evolve in indoor environments. For the vast majority of human evolutionary history, the baseline light environment was outdoor: high-intensity, spectrally broad, varying with time of day. The visual system calibrated its development accordingly.

What we have created in the past century and a half of industrialization and urbanization is a population that grows up spending the majority of its waking hours in environments with light levels that the human developmental system was never designed for. Myopia, in this frame, is not a disease in the conventional sense — it is a developmental response to conditions that differ from those under which the relevant biological calibration evolved.

This is a conceptual category that appears elsewhere in modern health: the mismatch between the environment the human body evolved for and the environment it now inhabits. Chronic sleep disruption, metabolic disease, chronic inflammation — all involve some version of this story. Myopia joins the list.

The preventive intervention is correspondingly ecological rather than pharmacological: more time in bright outdoor light during developmental years.

## What Is Being Done

The evidence has been sufficient to generate national public health responses in several East Asian countries. Taiwan introduced policies mandating daily outdoor time in schools beginning in 2010; myopia incidence subsequently declined. China has implemented similar programs at scale. Singapore has invested in outdoor school design and structured outdoor play time for primary school children.

Ophthalmological interventions exist for already-myopic children — low-dose atropine eye drops, orthokeratology contact lenses, and multifocal spectacle lenses have all been shown to slow progression with varying effect sizes. These are treatments for an already-developing condition, not prevention of the underlying environmental mismatch.

The architectural implication that has received the least attention is perhaps the most significant: school and daycare design that maximizes natural light and outdoor exposure during the hours when children's eyes are most developmentally active. A generation of school buildings optimized for weather protection and energy efficiency at the expense of natural light exposure may be contributing to the myopia epidemic in ways that are harder to quantify than screen time but no less real.

---

## The Number

One figure from the myopia literature deserves particular attention for what it implies. By 2050, on current trends, researchers project that approximately 50% of the global population will be myopic — roughly 4.7 billion people.

Myopia is not merely an inconvenience that glasses resolve. High myopia (severe nearsightedness) is a leading cause of visual impairment and blindness globally, through associated conditions including retinal detachment, glaucoma, macular degeneration, and cataracts. The epidemic-scale projection is therefore not a prediction of more people needing glasses; it is a projection of substantially increased population-level visual disability.

The intervention — more time outdoors, in bright light, during childhood — is not exotic, expensive, or technologically demanding. It is the environmental condition humans have always existed in, made newly scarce by the world we have built around ourselves.

The eye was calibrated for a world with more sky in it. We built a world with ceilings instead. The eye responded accordingly.
